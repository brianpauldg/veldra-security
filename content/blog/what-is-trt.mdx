# What Is TRT? A Complete Guide to Testosterone Replacement Therapy

Testosterone replacement therapy — commonly referred to as TRT — is a physician-supervised medical protocol designed to restore testosterone levels in men whose bodies no longer produce adequate amounts on their own. It is not a performance shortcut. It is not a trend. It is a clinical intervention with decades of published literature, prescribed when laboratory evidence and symptom presentation indicate a genuine hormonal deficit.

If you have spent the last several months or years feeling like something is off — your energy declining, your body composition shifting, your mental sharpness eroding — and standard explanations like stress or aging never quite satisfied you, TRT may be worth understanding. Not because it is a solution for everyone, but because informed patients make better decisions.

This guide covers what TRT is, how testosterone functions in the male body, who qualifies, what a supervised protocol looks like, and how to evaluate whether a provider is worth your trust.

## How Testosterone Works in the Body

To understand TRT, you first need to understand the system it is designed to support.

Testosterone is the primary androgen hormone in males. It is produced predominantly in the Leydig cells of the testes, with small contributions from the adrenal glands. But production does not happen in isolation — it is governed by a feedback loop known as the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis.

Here is the simplified version:

1. **The hypothalamus** releases gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) in pulsatile bursts.
2. **The pituitary gland** responds by secreting luteinizing hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH).
3. **LH signals the testes** to produce testosterone. FSH supports spermatogenesis.
4. **Rising testosterone levels** signal back to the hypothalamus and pituitary to reduce GnRH and LH output — completing the feedback loop.

This axis is elegant when it works. When it does not — whether due to primary testicular failure, secondary pituitary dysfunction, or the gradual age-related decline that begins around age 30 — the downstream consequences touch nearly every system in the body: musculoskeletal, metabolic, cognitive, cardiovascular, and reproductive.

Testosterone influences protein synthesis, red blood cell production, bone mineral density, fat distribution, mood regulation, and libido. When levels fall below the clinical threshold — generally defined as total testosterone below 300 ng/dL, though symptom presentation matters as much as the number — the cumulative effect can be significant.

## Signs That Testosterone May Be Low

Low testosterone does not announce itself with a single unmistakable symptom. It presents as a pattern — a gradual erosion across multiple domains that most men rationalize as normal aging, work stress, or poor sleep habits.

The clinical signs include persistent fatigue, declining libido, difficulty building or maintaining muscle mass, increased visceral body fat, brain fog, mood instability, disrupted sleep, and reduced recovery capacity. We cover all twelve commonly overlooked symptoms in detail in our guide to [low testosterone symptoms](/blog/low-testosterone-symptoms).

The challenge is that every one of these symptoms has alternative explanations. That is precisely why laboratory confirmation is essential before considering TRT. Symptoms alone are not diagnostic — they are the reason to investigate.

## Who Is a Candidate for TRT

TRT is not appropriate for every man who feels tired or wants to build muscle faster. Candidacy requires the intersection of three criteria:

### 1. Symptomatic Presentation

The patient must report symptoms consistent with testosterone deficiency. These typically include some combination of the signs described above — fatigue, body composition changes, cognitive decline, libido reduction, or mood disruption. A structured symptom assessment, often using validated tools like the ADAM questionnaire, helps quantify the clinical picture.

### 2. Laboratory Confirmation

Symptoms must be supported by bloodwork. The standard initial panel includes:

- **Total testosterone** (drawn in the morning, when levels peak)
- **Free testosterone** (the bioavailable fraction not bound to SHBG)
- **SHBG** (sex hormone-binding globulin)
- **LH and FSH** (to distinguish primary from secondary hypogonadism)
- **Estradiol** (to assess aromatization baseline)
- **CBC, CMP, lipid panel** (to establish metabolic baseline)
- **PSA** (prostate-specific antigen, for baseline screening)

Most clinical guidelines define low testosterone as total T below 300 ng/dL on two separate morning draws, though some practitioners consider levels below 400 ng/dL clinically relevant when accompanied by clear symptoms. For a deeper look at what your labs mean, see our guide to [TRT blood work](/blog/trt-blood-work).

### 3. Absence of Contraindications

Certain conditions may preclude TRT or require additional evaluation before initiation. These include untreated severe obstructive sleep apnea, erythrocytosis (elevated hematocrit), active or suspected prostate or breast cancer, and uncontrolled heart failure. A thorough medical history and physical evaluation are non-negotiable components of any responsible prescribing decision.

## Types of TRT Delivery

Testosterone can be administered through several routes. Each has distinct pharmacokinetic profiles, advantages, and limitations.

### Injectable Testosterone (Intramuscular or Subcutaneous)

Injectable testosterone — typically testosterone cypionate or enanthate — is the most common and cost-effective delivery method. It provides reliable, dose-adjustable testosterone delivery with well-characterized absorption profiles.

- **Frequency:** Typically administered one to two times per week
- **Advantages:** Precise dosing, consistent levels with split dosing, cost-effective, well-studied
- **Considerations:** Requires self-injection (most patients learn quickly), may cause injection site soreness

This is the primary delivery method used at Bloom Metabolics. Subcutaneous injection with small-gauge needles has become increasingly common and is generally well-tolerated. Split dosing — smaller, more frequent injections — helps maintain stable serum levels and may reduce estrogen conversion.

### Topical Testosterone (Gels and Creams)

Topical formulations are applied daily to the skin, typically on the shoulders, upper arms, or inner thighs.

- **Frequency:** Daily application
- **Advantages:** Non-invasive, no needles
- **Considerations:** Risk of transference to partners or children through skin contact, variable absorption rates, higher cost, daily compliance requirement

### Testosterone Pellets

Subcutaneous pellets are implanted in a minor in-office procedure and release testosterone over three to six months.

- **Frequency:** Every 3-6 months
- **Advantages:** Long-acting, no daily or weekly administration
- **Considerations:** Requires an in-office procedure for insertion, dose cannot be easily adjusted once implanted, risk of pellet extrusion

### Nasal and Oral Formulations

Newer delivery methods include nasal gels and oral testosterone undecanoate. These offer convenience but have more limited long-term outcome data and may not achieve the stable levels most protocols require.

## What a TRT Protocol Looks Like

A well-designed TRT protocol is not a single prescription — it is an iterative clinical process. At Bloom Metabolics, the process follows a structured pathway:

### Step 2: Diagnostic Lab Work

Based on the consultation, a laboratory panel is ordered. This is the diagnostic foundation — no responsible provider prescribes TRT without current bloodwork. If you have recent labs from another provider, those may be reviewed, but confirmatory draws are often recommended.

### Step 3: Protocol Design

If labs confirm testosterone deficiency and the clinical picture supports treatment, your physician designs a personalized protocol. This includes:

- **Testosterone dose and frequency** (calibrated to your levels, body weight, and response goals)
- **Delivery method** (typically injectable testosterone cypionate)
- **Adjunct medications** if indicated (discussed below)
- **Monitoring schedule** (follow-up labs and check-ins)

### Step 4: Medication Delivery and Initiation

Medications are shipped directly to your door from a licensed pharmacy. Injection training and protocol guidance are provided so you can self-administer with confidence.

### Step 5: Ongoing Monitoring and Optimization

TRT is not set-and-forget. Follow-up bloodwork is typically drawn at 6-8 weeks after initiation, then at regular intervals (every 3-6 months during the first year, then biannually or as clinically indicated). Dose adjustments, ancillary medication changes, and protocol refinements are made based on lab trends and symptom response.

## The Role of HCG and Anastrozole as Protocol Adjuncts

Testosterone replacement, by its nature, suppresses the HPG axis. Exogenous testosterone signals the hypothalamus and pituitary to reduce LH and FSH output, which in turn reduces endogenous testicular function. Two adjunct medications are commonly used to manage the downstream effects of this suppression.

### HCG (Human Chorionic Gonadotropin)

HCG mimics the action of LH at the testicular level. When administered alongside TRT, it may help:

- Maintain intratesticular testosterone production
- Preserve testicular volume
- Support fertility by maintaining spermatogenesis

HCG is particularly relevant for men who wish to preserve fertility while on TRT, as exogenous testosterone alone is associated with significant suppression of sperm production. For a detailed discussion, see our guide to [HCG and TRT](/blog/hcg-and-trt).

### Anastrozole

Anastrozole is an aromatase inhibitor — it reduces the conversion of testosterone to estradiol. Some men on TRT experience elevated estrogen levels due to increased aromatization, which may contribute to water retention, mood changes, or gynecomastia.

Anastrozole is not universally prescribed. Its use is guided by lab values (specifically estradiol levels) and symptom presentation. Many modern protocols favor dose and frequency adjustments to manage estrogen before introducing an AI, as over-suppression of estradiol carries its own risks — estrogen plays important roles in bone health, cardiovascular function, and libido even in men.

## Monitoring and Lab Work Requirements

Ongoing monitoring is what separates responsible TRT management from reckless prescribing. The following markers are typically tracked:

- **Total and free testosterone** — to confirm levels are in the therapeutic range
- **Estradiol** — to monitor aromatization
- **Hematocrit and hemoglobin** — testosterone may stimulate erythropoiesis; elevated hematocrit requires intervention
- **PSA** — monitored at baseline and periodically for prostate health screening
- **Lipid panel** — to track cardiovascular risk markers
- **Metabolic panel** — liver and kidney function
- **SHBG** — to assess binding protein changes

Your provider should be reviewing these values at every follow-up and adjusting your protocol accordingly. If your provider prescribes TRT and does not require follow-up labs, that is a red flag. Learn more about what your bloodwork means in our [TRT blood work guide](/blog/trt-blood-work).

## Common Questions About TRT

### Is TRT the same as steroids?

TRT uses the same molecule — testosterone — that is used in anabolic steroid protocols, but the intent, dosing, and supervision are fundamentally different. TRT aims to restore physiological levels (typically 600-1000 ng/dL total testosterone). Supraphysiological doses used for performance enhancement carry substantially different risk profiles. TRT is a medical treatment for a diagnosed deficiency, prescribed and monitored by a physician.

### Will TRT affect my fertility?

Exogenous testosterone suppresses the HPG axis, which commonly results in reduced or absent sperm production. This effect is generally reversible upon discontinuation, but recovery timelines vary. Men who wish to preserve fertility should discuss adjunct therapies like HCG with their provider before starting TRT. This is a critical conversation to have upfront.

### How long does it take to notice results?

Response timelines vary by individual, but published literature and clinical experience suggest a general pattern: improvements in energy and mood may be noticed within the first 3-6 weeks, libido changes within 3-12 weeks, and body composition changes over 3-6 months with consistent training and nutrition. We outline the full expected timeline in our [TRT benefits timeline](/blog/trt-benefits-timeline) article. Individual results vary.

### Can I stop TRT once I start?

TRT can be discontinued, but doing so requires medical supervision. The HPG axis may take weeks to months to recover endogenous production, and some men experience a temporary period of symptoms during this recovery. Your provider can design a tapering and recovery protocol if discontinuation is desired.

### Is TRT safe long-term?

Long-term safety data on TRT has grown substantially over the past decade. The TRAVERSE trial (2023), one of the largest randomized controlled trials on TRT, did not find an increased risk of major adverse cardiovascular events in men with hypogonadism and pre-existing or high risk for cardiovascular disease. That said, TRT requires ongoing monitoring — particularly of hematocrit, PSA, and cardiovascular risk factors — to maintain safety over time. Individual risk profiles should be discussed with your physician.

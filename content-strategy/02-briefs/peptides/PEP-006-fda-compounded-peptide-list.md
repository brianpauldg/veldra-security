## The Angle

The FDA maintains several overlapping lists that determine what can and cannot be compounded. Most patients — and many providers — don't know how to read them. There's the bulk drug substances under evaluation list, the bulk drug substances for which there is a clinical need list, the withdrawn or removed list, and various guidance documents that modify interpretation. This piece is a reference guide: here are the lists, here's how they interact, here's how to interpret what you find. Straightforward utility. The kind of piece that gets bookmarked.

### H2: How the Lists Apply Differently to 503A and 503B
- 503B outsourcing facilities: directly bound by the FDA's bulk drug substance lists
- 503A pharmacies: governed by a different section of DQSA with different list requirements
- The practical effect: a substance restricted for 503B may have different status under 503A
- Cross-reference to PEP-002 for the full 503A/503B framework explanation

### H2: What the Lists Don't Tell You
- Quality of the compounding pharmacy is not reflected in substance eligibility
- A substance being "permitted" doesn't mean every compounder makes it well
- State-level regulations add another layer not captured in federal lists
- The lists are a starting point for evaluation, not an endpoint

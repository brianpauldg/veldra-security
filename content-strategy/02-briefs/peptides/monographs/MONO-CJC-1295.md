## Identity & Classification
- Full name: CJC-1295 (also known as Modified GRF 1-29, Drug Affinity Complex GRF)
- Peptide sequence: 29 amino acids — a modified analog of growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH) 1-29 with amino acid substitutions at positions 2, 8, 15, and 27 to resist DPP-IV degradation
- Classification: Growth hormone secretagogue / GHRH analog
- Discovery: Developed by ConjuChem Biotechnologies (now Adjuvance Technologies). Two forms exist: CJC-1295 DAC (with Drug Affinity Complex — binds albumin for extended half-life) and CJC-1295 without DAC (also called Modified GRF 1-29 or Mod GRF)
- Common alternate names: Mod GRF 1-29 (without DAC), CJC-1295 DAC, Modified GRF, Tesamorelin-adjacent (different peptide but same pathway family)

## Mechanism of Action
- GHRH receptor agonism: Binds the GHRH receptor on anterior pituitary somatotroph cells, stimulating growth hormone (GH) release
- Pulsatile GH release: Amplifies natural GH pulsatility rather than creating continuous supraphysiological levels (particularly without DAC form)
- DAC vs non-DAC: The DAC version binds albumin, extending half-life from ~30 minutes to ~8 days. This changes the pharmacokinetic profile substantially — DAC creates more sustained GH elevation, non-DAC preserves natural pulsatility
- IGF-1 elevation: Secondary to GH increase, IGF-1 levels rise, mediating many of the downstream anabolic and metabolic effects
- Established: GHRH receptor binding and GH release stimulation are well-characterized
- Hypothesized: Body composition improvement, sleep quality enhancement, recovery acceleration
- Incomplete: Long-term safety of chronic GH axis stimulation, optimal dosing for different therapeutic goals, DAC vs non-DAC clinical outcome comparison in humans

## Bloom Evidence Tier Rating
Tier 2 — Well-understood mechanism with established receptor pharmacology. Human pharmacokinetic data exists (ConjuChem clinical trials). Body composition and GH/IGF-1 elevation data exists in human studies. However, long-term safety and clinical outcome data beyond biomarker changes is limited.

Justification: CJC-1295 has more human pharmacokinetic data than most peptides in this library, largely due to ConjuChem's clinical development program. GH and IGF-1 elevation in humans has been demonstrated. The gap is between biomarker elevation (GH/IGF-1 levels go up) and clinical outcomes (does that produce meaningful body composition, recovery, or quality-of-life improvements in a way that justifies the intervention?).

## Human Clinical Research
- Teichman SL, et al. "Prolonged stimulation of growth hormone (GH) and insulin-like growth factor I secretion by CJC-1295, a long-acting analog of GH-releasing hormone, in healthy adults." J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2006;91(3):799-805. PMID: 16352683 — Phase I/II study demonstrating dose-dependent GH and IGF-1 elevation in healthy adults with CJC-1295 DAC.
- ConjuChem clinical program: Phase II data showed sustained IGF-1 elevation for 6-14 days after single injection (DAC form). Program was discontinued (company financial issues, not safety signal — though one unexplained death in an unrelated trial was noted by FDA).
- Limitations: Clinical development was halted before Phase III. The most cited human data comes from short-term pharmacokinetic studies, not long-term outcome trials.

## Animal & Mechanistic Research
- GH axis stimulation well-characterized in animal models
- Body composition changes (reduced fat mass, increased lean mass) observed in animal models with chronic GHRH analog administration
- Sleep architecture effects: GH secretagogues as a class have been associated with improved slow-wave sleep in some animal and human studies
- Extrapolation limits: The DAC vs non-DAC distinction is critical — most clinical data is with DAC, most compounded product is without DAC

## Regulatory Status (as of May 2026)
- Current FDA category status: Under FDA evaluation for compounding eligibility. CJC-1295 has been included in FDA scrutiny of GH secretagogue peptides.
- 503A compoundability: Available through some 503A compounding pharmacies; state-level variance applies
- 503B compoundability: Restricted or under review
- Recent regulatory changes: FDA has increased scrutiny of GH secretagogue peptides as a class
- Pending FDA actions: Category evaluation ongoing
- International: Not approved as a pharmaceutical in any major jurisdiction. WADA-prohibited.

## Clinical Considerations
- Theoretical benefits: GH optimization, body composition improvement, recovery support, sleep quality enhancement
- Known contraindications: Active malignancy (GH/IGF-1 axis stimulation is contraindicated), diabetes (GH effects on glucose homeostasis), history of pituitary tumors
- Drug interactions: Insulin and oral hypoglycemics (GH is counter-regulatory to insulin), glucocorticoids (may attenuate GH response)
- Monitoring: IGF-1, fasting glucose, HbA1c, body composition assessment
- Population-specific: Age-related GH decline provides the theoretical rationale; cancer screening must be current before GH axis stimulation

## Differentiation Angle
Most CJC-1295 content conflates the DAC and non-DAC forms as if they are interchangeable. They are not — an 8-day half-life vs a 30-minute half-life produces fundamentally different pharmacokinetic profiles. This monograph draws the distinction clearly and addresses the discontinued clinical program honestly rather than omitting it.

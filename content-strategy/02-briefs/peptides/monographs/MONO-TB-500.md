## Identity & Classification
- Full name: Thymosin Beta-4 (Tβ4), commonly sold as TB-500 (a synthetic fragment/analog)
- Peptide sequence: 43 amino acids (full Tβ4); TB-500 is a synthetic peptide designed to replicate the active region
- Classification: Actin-sequestering peptide / tissue repair peptide
- Discovery: Thymosin Beta-4 was first isolated from calf thymus by Dr. Allan Goldstein in the 1960s-70s as part of thymosin fraction 5 research. TB-500 emerged later as a commercially available synthetic analog.
- Common alternate names: Thymosin Beta 4, Tβ4, TB4, TB-500

## Mechanism of Action
- Actin regulation: Tβ4 is the primary intracellular G-actin sequestering peptide, regulating actin polymerization which is fundamental to cell motility, wound healing, and tissue repair
- Cell migration: Promotes endothelial cell and keratinocyte migration — critical steps in wound healing and angiogenesis
- Anti-inflammatory properties: Reduces inflammatory cytokines and modulates the NF-κB pathway in animal models
- Cardiac repair signals: Reactivation of epicardial progenitor cells in mouse cardiac injury models (Srivastava lab, UT Southwestern)
- Established: Actin-sequestering function is well-established biochemistry. Cell migration promotion is demonstrated in vitro and in animal models.
- Hypothesized: Systemic tissue healing, cardiac repair, hair growth promotion
- Incomplete: Human pharmacokinetics with exogenous administration, optimal dosing, whether exogenous TB-500 reaches intracellular targets

## Bloom Evidence Tier Rating
Tier 3 — Strong mechanistic understanding of actin biology and cell migration. Animal model data for wound healing and cardiac repair is interesting but varied. Human clinical data is limited. The distinction between Tβ4 (the endogenous protein) and TB-500 (the synthetic product) must be clearly drawn — most research uses Tβ4, not TB-500.

Justification: The fundamental biology of thymosin beta-4 is well-established. However, most preclinical research uses the full 43-amino-acid Tβ4, while the commercially available product (TB-500) is a synthetic analog. The degree to which TB-500 replicates Tβ4's biological activity is assumed but not rigorously validated in comparative studies. Human clinical trials are limited to ophthalmic applications (RegeneRx Biopharmaceuticals).

## Human Clinical Research
- RegeneRx Biopharmaceuticals: Phase II trials of Tβ4 (RGN-259) for dry eye syndrome and neurotrophic keratopathy — ophthalmic topical application, not systemic
- No published human interventional trials of systemically administered TB-500 or Tβ4 for musculoskeletal healing as of May 2026
- Equine research: TB-500 has been studied (and banned) in the horse racing industry for tendon healing — these animal studies inform but do not substitute for human data
- Limitations: The most advanced human clinical programs use Tβ4 for eye conditions, not TB-500 for systemic tissue healing

## Animal & Mechanistic Research
- Cardiac repair: Mouse models showing cardiac progenitor cell reactivation after myocardial infarction (Srivastava lab)
- Wound healing: Accelerated dermal wound closure in mouse and rat models
- Tendon repair: Equine tendon healing data
- Hair growth: Mouse models showing follicle stem cell activation
- Extrapolation limits: The Tβ4 vs TB-500 distinction is critical. Most published research uses pharmaceutical-grade Tβ4, not commercial TB-500. Bioequivalence is assumed, not proven.

## Regulatory Status (as of May 2026)
- Current FDA category status: Tβ4/TB-500 has been placed under FDA evaluation for compounding eligibility
- 503A compoundability: Status varies by state — some jurisdictions permit, others have restricted
- 503B compoundability: Restricted or under review
- Recent regulatory changes: FDA enforcement actions in 2024-2025 included TB-500 in broader peptide compounding scrutiny
- Pending FDA actions: Category evaluation ongoing
- International: Banned in professional and amateur sports by WADA (World Anti-Doping Agency)

## Clinical Considerations
- Theoretical benefits: Tissue repair (tendon, muscle, cardiac), wound healing, anti-inflammatory effects
- Known contraindications: Active malignancy (theoretical concern — cell migration promotion could theoretically affect tumor metastasis)
- Drug interactions: No established drug interactions (insufficient human data)
- Monitoring: Standard inflammation markers, injury-specific assessment
- Population-specific: Cancer history is a theoretical concern due to cell migration mechanism; athletes should be aware of WADA prohibition

## Differentiation Angle
The critical distinction between endogenous Tβ4 and commercial TB-500 is absent from virtually all patient-facing TB-500 content. Vendor pages cite Tβ4 research as if it directly validates TB-500 products. This monograph draws the line clearly: the biology of thymosin beta-4 is established, but the assumption that commercial TB-500 replicates it is unvalidated. No existing source makes this distinction for patients.

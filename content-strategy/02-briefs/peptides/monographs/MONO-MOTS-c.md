## Identity & Classification
- Full name: Mitochondrial Open Reading Frame of the 12S rRNA Type-c (MOTS-c)
- Peptide sequence: 16 amino acids (MRWQEMGYIFYPRKLR)
- Classification: Mitochondrial-derived peptide (MDP)
- Discovery: Identified in 2015 by Dr. Changhan David Lee's laboratory at the University of Southern California. First mitochondrial-derived peptide shown to regulate nuclear gene expression.
- Common alternate names: MOTS-c peptide, mitochondrial peptide

## Mechanism of Action
- Primary target: AMPK pathway activation — MOTS-c activates AMP-activated protein kinase, a master metabolic regulator
- Folate-methionine cycle interaction: MOTS-c inhibits the folate cycle, leading to AICAR accumulation, which activates AMPK
- Nuclear translocation: Under metabolic stress, MOTS-c translocates from cytoplasm to nucleus and regulates adaptive nuclear gene expression — a novel mechanism for a mitochondrial-derived peptide
- Downstream effects: Glucose regulation, fatty acid oxidation, improved insulin sensitivity (in animal models)
- Established: AMPK activation mechanism and nuclear translocation are well-characterized in cell and animal models
- Hypothesized: Exercise-mimetic properties, metabolic stress adaptation in humans
- Incomplete: Human pharmacokinetic data, optimal dosing, long-term safety profile, tissue-specific distribution

## Bloom Evidence Tier Rating
Tier 2 — Strong preclinical evidence with mechanistic clarity. Limited but emerging human data. The AMPK activation pathway is well-established. Human clinical validation is early-stage. Evidence supports biological plausibility but not clinical efficacy claims.

Justification: Multiple independent research groups have confirmed the mechanism. The USC group has published extensively, and independent replication exists for core findings. However, the human clinical trial pipeline is nascent. No completed Phase III trials. The gap between "activates same pathways as exercise" and "replaces exercise" remains unproven in humans.

## Human Clinical Research
- Lee C, et al. "MOTS-c: A Novel Mitochondrial-Derived Peptide Regulating Muscle and Fat Metabolism." J Am Geriatr Soc. 2016. — Foundational discovery paper establishing MOTS-c's role in metabolic regulation. Primarily cell culture and animal model data, but includes human correlational data showing MOTS-c levels decline with age.
- Kim SJ, et al. "The mitochondrial-derived peptide MOTS-c is a regulator of plasma metabolites and enhances insulin sensitivity." Physiol Rep. 2019. — Demonstrated MOTS-c improves insulin sensitivity and glucose metabolism in mouse models. Sample size limited to animal models.
- Reynolds JC, et al. "MOTS-c is an exercise-induced mitochondrial-encoded regulator of age-dependent physical decline and muscle homeostasis." Nat Commun. 2021. — Key paper showing exercise increases endogenous MOTS-c levels and that exogenous MOTS-c improves physical performance in aged mice. Provides the exercise-mimetic hypothesis foundation.
- Clinical relevance: No completed human interventional trials as of 2026. Observational data shows declining MOTS-c levels correlate with age and metabolic dysfunction. Interventional human data is the critical gap.
- Limitations: Most data from a concentrated research group (USC). Independent replication of interventional effects is limited. Animal-to-human extrapolation carries standard caveats.

## Animal & Mechanistic Research
- Extensive mouse model data showing improved glucose homeostasis, enhanced exercise capacity, and protection against diet-induced obesity
- Nuclear translocation mechanism is novel and well-characterized at the cellular level
- Age-related decline in endogenous MOTS-c production documented in both rodent and human observational studies
- Extrapolation limits: Mouse metabolic models have historically shown poor translation to human metabolic outcomes. The exercise-mimetic framing is based on shared pathway activation, not demonstrated equivalence.

## Regulatory Status (as of May 2026)
- Current FDA category status: Not currently categorized under FDA bulk drug substance evaluation framework (MDPs are a relatively new category)
- 503A compoundability: Available through some 503A compounding pharmacies in certain states; state-level variance applies
- 503B compoundability: Not listed on 503B bulk drug substance lists
- Recent regulatory changes: No specific FDA enforcement actions targeting MOTS-c as of May 2026
- Pending FDA actions: None publicly announced
- International: Not scheduled or restricted under TGA (Australia) or EMA (Europe) frameworks as of May 2026

## Clinical Considerations
- Theoretical benefits: Metabolic optimization, insulin sensitivity support, exercise capacity enhancement, age-related metabolic decline mitigation
- Known contraindications: None established in human studies (insufficient human data to characterize)
- Drug interactions: Theoretical concern with AMPK-modulating drugs (metformin) — additive AMPK activation. No interaction studies performed.
- Monitoring: Blood glucose, insulin sensitivity markers, metabolic panels recommended during any peptide use
- Population-specific: Age-related decline in endogenous MOTS-c suggests older populations may have greater theoretical benefit; however, this population also has the least safety data

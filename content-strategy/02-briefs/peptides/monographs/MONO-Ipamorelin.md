## Identity & Classification
- Full name: Ipamorelin (NNC 26-0161)
- Peptide sequence: Pentapeptide (Aib-His-D-2-Nal-D-Phe-Lys-NH2)
- Classification: Growth hormone secretagogue / Growth hormone releasing peptide (GHRP) / Ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a) agonist
- Discovery: Developed by Novo Nordisk in the 1990s. Identified as a selective GH secretagogue with minimal effects on cortisol, prolactin, and ACTH — distinguishing it from earlier GHRPs like GHRP-6 and GHRP-2.
- Common alternate names: NNC 26-0161, Ipamorelin acetate

## Mechanism of Action
- GHS-R1a agonism: Binds the ghrelin receptor (growth hormone secretagogue receptor type 1a) on anterior pituitary somatotrophs, triggering GH release
- Selectivity: Unlike GHRP-6 and GHRP-2, Ipamorelin does not significantly elevate cortisol, prolactin, or ACTH at GH-stimulating doses — this selectivity is its primary differentiator
- Pulsatile GH release: Stimulates GH release in a dose-dependent manner while preserving natural pulsatility
- Synergy with GHRH analogs: When combined with CJC-1295 (a GHRH analog), Ipamorelin and CJC-1295 activate complementary receptor pathways — Ipamorelin via ghrelin receptor, CJC-1295 via GHRH receptor — theoretically producing greater GH release than either alone
- Established: GHS-R1a binding, selective GH release without cortisol/prolactin elevation (demonstrated in human studies)
- Hypothesized: Body composition improvement, sleep enhancement, recovery acceleration
- Incomplete: Long-term clinical outcomes, optimal dosing for non-GH-deficiency applications

## Bloom Evidence Tier Rating
Tier 2 — Selective GH secretagogue mechanism well-characterized in human studies. Novo Nordisk's clinical development program produced human pharmacodynamic data confirming selectivity. However, like CJC-1295, the gap between GH elevation and meaningful clinical outcomes has not been bridged by completed Phase III trials.

Justification: Ipamorelin has the strongest selectivity data of any GHRP — the lack of cortisol and prolactin elevation at therapeutic GH-stimulating doses is demonstrated in multiple human studies. This is a meaningful clinical advantage. However, the clinical development program was discontinued by Novo Nordisk (reportedly due to strategic portfolio decisions, not safety concerns), and no Phase III outcome data exists.

## Human Clinical Research
- Raun K, et al. "Ipamorelin, the first selective growth hormone secretagogue." Eur J Endocrinol. 1998;139(5):552-61. PMID: 9849822 — Foundational selectivity study demonstrating GH elevation without cortisol/prolactin changes in human subjects.
- Gobburu JV, et al. "Pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic modeling of ipamorelin, a growth hormone releasing peptide." J Clin Pharmacol. 1999;39(1):67-75. PMID: 9987703 — Human PK/PD data establishing dose-response relationships.
- Beck DE, et al. Phase II trial for postoperative ileus — a non-GH application providing additional human safety data
- Limitations: Clinical development discontinued before Phase III for GH optimization indications. Post-operative ileus trials provide human safety data but do not address the primary use case.

## Animal & Mechanistic Research
- Dose-dependent GH release without cortisol/prolactin elevation confirmed in multiple animal species
- GI motility effects characterized in rat models
- Body composition effects observed in animal models with chronic administration
- Extrapolation limits: The selectivity advantage is human-validated, but the clinical significance of that selectivity for long-term GH optimization remains unproven

## Regulatory Status (as of May 2026)
- Current FDA category status: Under FDA evaluation for compounding eligibility alongside other GH secretagogues
- 503A compoundability: Available through some 503A compounding pharmacies; state-level variance
- 503B compoundability: Restricted or under review
- Recent regulatory changes: Included in broader FDA scrutiny of GH secretagogue peptides
- Pending FDA actions: Category evaluation ongoing
- International: WADA-prohibited. Not approved as a pharmaceutical in any major jurisdiction.

## Clinical Considerations
- Theoretical benefits: Selective GH optimization without cortisol/prolactin perturbation, body composition support, recovery, sleep quality
- Known contraindications: Active malignancy, diabetes/glucose impairment, pituitary tumors
- Drug interactions: Insulin and oral hypoglycemics (GH counter-regulatory effects), glucocorticoids
- Monitoring: IGF-1, fasting glucose, HbA1c
- Population-specific: The selectivity advantage may be most relevant for patients sensitive to cortisol elevation or prolactin-related side effects

## The Founder Voice Hook
"Ipamorelin earned its reputation for a simple reason: it does one thing cleanly. It stimulates GH without dragging cortisol and prolactin along for the ride. That selectivity is real — it is demonstrated in human studies, not just animal models. But selectivity is a pharmacodynamic property, not a clinical outcome. The question is not whether Ipamorelin elevates GH cleanly. It does. The question is whether that clean GH elevation produces the body composition and recovery outcomes that patients are seeking. That is a harder question, and one the research has not fully answered."

## Differentiation Angle
Most Ipamorelin content frames selectivity as a settled clinical advantage. This monograph distinguishes between selectivity as a pharmacodynamic property (proven) and selectivity as a clinical outcome predictor (unproven). The Novo Nordisk discontinuation is discussed honestly — strategic portfolio decision, not safety signal — rather than omitted.

## Identity & Classification
- Full name: Glycyl-L-Histidyl-L-Lysine:Copper(II) (GHK-Cu)
- Peptide sequence: Tripeptide (Gly-His-Lys) complexed with copper(II) ion
- Classification: Copper tripeptide complex / wound-healing peptide
- Discovery: First isolated from human plasma in 1973 by Dr. Loren Pickart. Initially identified as a factor in human plasma that caused aged liver tissue to synthesize proteins like younger tissue.
- Common alternate names: Copper peptide, copper tripeptide-1, GHK copper complex

## Mechanism of Action
- Copper binding: GHK-Cu delivers bioavailable copper to tissues, which is essential for multiple enzymatic processes
- Gene expression effects: Modulates expression of approximately 4,000 human genes — upregulates genes involved in tissue repair and collagen synthesis, downregulates genes associated with inflammation and tissue destruction
- Wound healing pathways: Stimulates collagen I and III synthesis, decorin production, glycosaminoglycan accumulation, and angiogenesis
- Anti-inflammatory signaling: Reduces pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-6) in cell and animal models
- Established: Dermatological mechanisms well-characterized (topical applications have decades of research)
- Hypothesized: Systemic anti-fibrotic effects, neuroprotective properties, lung tissue remodeling
- Incomplete: Systemic bioavailability data, optimal systemic dosing, tissue-specific distribution with parenteral administration

## Bloom Evidence Tier Rating
Tier 2 — Strong in vitro and topical evidence with nearly 50 years of published literature. The dermatological research base is robust. Systemic (injectable) applications have weaker evidence. The distinction between topical and systemic evidence quality is critical.

Justification: GHK-Cu has one of the longest research histories of any peptide in this library. Topical wound healing and dermatological applications are well-supported by human studies. However, the systemic administration evidence relies primarily on in vitro and animal data. The gene expression data is compelling but derived largely from computational genomics (Dr. Pickart's later work), not interventional human trials.

## Human Clinical Research
- Pickart L, et al. Multiple studies from 1973-2020 documenting GHK-Cu's wound healing and skin remodeling properties in human tissue.
- Leyden JJ, et al. Studies on topical GHK-Cu formulations showing improvements in skin density, firmness, and fine lines in human subjects.
- Topical clinical data: Multiple human trials demonstrating wound closure acceleration, post-surgical healing improvement, and dermatological outcomes
- Systemic clinical data: No published human interventional trials of parenteral (injected) GHK-Cu as of May 2026
- Limitations: The strongest human data is topical. Extrapolating from topical to systemic applications requires caution.

## Animal & Mechanistic Research
- Extensive wound healing data in animal models across multiple species
- Tissue remodeling/anti-fibrotic effects observed in lung and liver tissue (animal models)
- Neuroprotective signals in rat models of neurotoxicity
- Gene expression profiling showing broad effects on tissue repair genes (computational analysis)
- Extrapolation limits: Topical to systemic extrapolation is the primary caveat. A peptide that works applied to skin may behave differently when administered systemically.

## Regulatory Status (as of May 2026)
- Current FDA category status: Not specifically categorized under FDA bulk drug substance evaluation framework for injectable use; topical formulations available as cosmetic ingredients
- 503A compoundability: Available through some 503A compounding pharmacies for injectable formulations; state-level variance applies
- 503B compoundability: Not listed on standard 503B bulk drug substance lists for injectable use
- Recent regulatory changes: No specific FDA enforcement actions targeting GHK-Cu as of May 2026
- Pending FDA actions: None publicly announced
- International: Available as a cosmetic ingredient globally; injectable regulatory status varies by jurisdiction

## Clinical Considerations
- Theoretical benefits: Tissue repair, wound healing support, anti-inflammatory effects, skin health, potential anti-fibrotic properties
- Known contraindications: Copper sensitivity, Wilson's disease (copper metabolism disorder)
- Drug interactions: Theoretical concern with copper chelators (penicillamine, trientine)
- Monitoring: Serum copper levels, ceruloplasmin (with chronic use), liver function
- Population-specific: Patients with existing wound healing impairment may have the strongest theoretical rationale; patients with copper metabolism disorders must avoid

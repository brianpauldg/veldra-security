## The Angle

Longevity peptide marketing has outpaced the science by a wide margin. Instagram carousels promise "cellular rejuvenation" and "biological age reversal" from compounds that have, at best, shown interesting signals in mouse models. That doesn't mean the research is worthless — some of it is genuinely compelling. It means the honest conversation requires separating three categories: what's promising, what's overhyped, and what the gerontology literature actually supports. This piece is that separation, conducted without cynicism and without salesmanship.

### H2: Peptides With Genuine Research Interest in Longevity
- **H3: MOTS-c — Mitochondrial Signaling**
  - Endogenous mitochondrial-derived peptide
  - AMPK activation and metabolic homeostasis data
  - The exercise mimetic research and its limitations
  - Evidence level: strong preclinical, very early human
  - Cross-reference to PEP-003
- **H3: Epitalon — Telomerase Activation**
  - Dr. Vladimir Khavinson's research at the St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology
  - Telomerase activation in cell culture models
  - The telomere-cancer tension: why activating telomerase is not straightforwardly "good"
  - Evidence level: preclinical with some small human studies of mixed quality
- **H3: GHK-Cu — Tissue Remodeling and Gene Expression**
  - Decades of dermatological research
  - Gene expression modulation data suggesting broad regulatory effects
  - Evidence level: strong mechanistic characterization, limited systemic longevity data
  - Cross-reference to PEP-004
- **H3: Thymosin Alpha-1 — Immune Modulation**
  - The only peptide in this survey with an FDA-approved analog in some markets (Zadaxin, approved outside US)
  - Immune senescence and the aging immune system
  - COVID-era research interest and what it showed
  - Evidence level: strongest regulatory track record among longevity-adjacent peptides
- **H3: Humanin — The Other Mitochondrial Peptide**
  - Discovered in the context of Alzheimer's research
  - Cytoprotective effects in preclinical neurodegeneration models
  - Less commercially hyped than MOTS-c, arguably as interesting

### H2: Peptides That Are Overhyped Relative to Evidence
- A careful, non-dismissive assessment of compounds where marketing claims significantly exceed published evidence
- Common patterns in overhype: extrapolating from mechanism to outcome, citing animal doses as if they translate to human protocols, conflating "biologically active" with "clinically useful"
- Why overhype damages the credibility of legitimate research
- No specific compound shaming — focus on the patterns rather than naming and blaming

### H2: What the Gerontology Community Actually Thinks
- How longevity researchers at institutions like the Buck Institute, Karolinska, and the NIH's NIA view peptide therapy
- The gap between clinical geroscience and the consumer longevity market
- Why most academic researchers are cautiously interested but professionally distant from the peptide therapy industry
- The funding problem: who pays for peptide longevity trials when the molecules can't be patented

### H2: The Honest Assessment
- Some peptides are addressing real biological mechanisms relevant to aging
- The evidence base is insufficient for therapeutic claims in nearly every case
- The most promising peptides are the ones with the most boring timelines — decades of incremental research
- One nuanced observation: the longevity peptide market's excesses may actually slow legitimate research by eroding institutional credibility and attracting regulatory scrutiny to the entire compounding space
- Patients deserve better than either dismissal or salesmanship

### H2: What a Responsible Longevity Practice Looks Like
- Prioritizing interventions with established evidence: exercise, sleep, metabolic health markers, standard screening
- Where peptides might eventually fit in a hierarchy of evidence-based interventions
- Why the foundation matters more than the frontier
- The patience required to do this right

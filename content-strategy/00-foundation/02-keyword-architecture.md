## Strategic Framework: Why Semantic Depth Beats Keyword Density in 2026 Medical SEO

Google's algorithm evolution from 2022-2024 fundamentally changed how medical content is evaluated and ranked. The old model — target a primary keyword, achieve a specific keyword density, build backlinks — has been replaced by a semantic understanding model that rewards topical authority, clinical vocabulary depth, and content comprehensiveness.

### The Algorithm Updates That Changed the Game

**Helpful Content Update (August 2022, September 2023):** Introduced a site-wide signal that downranks domains with substantial content that appears to exist primarily for search engine ranking rather than user value. For medical content, this means articles that are keyword-optimized but clinically shallow are now actively penalized. The update rewards content that demonstrates genuine expertise — measured by clinical vocabulary breadth, accurate mechanism-of-action explanations, and honest evidence assessment.

**March 2024 Core Update:** The largest core update in Google's history, explicitly targeting "low-quality content" and "content created primarily for search rankings." This update enhanced Google's ability to distinguish between content written by someone who understands a medical topic and content written by someone who researched it for an article. The signal is vocabulary: a clinician naturally uses terms like "SHBG," "equilibrium dialysis," and "intratesticular testosterone" because those are the concepts. A content writer uses them because they appeared in the SEO brief. Google's neural matching increasingly distinguishes between these.

**August 2024 Core Update:** Further refinement of helpful content signals with increased weight on E-E-A-T for YMYL queries. Sites with genuine clinical authorship saw ranking improvements. Sites with commodity health content saw declines regardless of backlink profile.

### What This Means for Bloom's Keyword Strategy

Bloom does not target keywords. Bloom claims concept territories. Each territory is a clinical phenomenon that Bloom names, defines, and owns through comprehensive, clinically deep content. The keyword is a byproduct of the territory claim, not the objective.

The Semantic Keyword Map in each brief defines three layers:
1. **Primary keyword** — the search query the article answers
2. **Secondary keywords** — related queries the article naturally addresses
3. **Latent semantic terms** — the clinical vocabulary that signals topical expertise to Google's neural matching. This is where Bloom wins. A TRT article that uses "SHBG," "Leydig cells," "gonadotropin," and "intratesticular testosterone" naturally signals that the author understands the endocrine system, not just the search query.

### What We Are NOT Doing

The following SEO practices are explicitly prohibited in all Bloom content production:

- **Keyword stuffing** — forcing a primary keyword into content at an unnatural frequency. No density targets. Use the keyword where it appears naturally.
- **Doorway pages** — creating multiple thin pages targeting slight keyword variations (e.g., separate pages for "low testosterone symptoms men" and "signs of low testosterone males"). One comprehensive article per search intent cluster.
- **Thin content** — publishing articles under 1,500 words that exist primarily to capture a keyword position. Every Bloom article must provide substantive clinical value.
- **AI-content-without-human-review** — all AI-assisted content must be reviewed and edited by the clinical team (Brian DeGuzman, RN) and medically reviewed (Dr. Napolitano, MD) before publication. AI is a drafting tool, not an author.
- **Link schemes** — purchasing links, excessive link exchanges, or manipulative anchor text strategies.
- **Hidden text or cloaking** — no content visible to crawlers but hidden from users.
- **Programmatic pages without clinical value** — if Bloom builds city pages or condition pages, each must contain unique clinical content, not template filler.
- **Cannibalization** — two articles targeting the same primary keyword. Each keyword maps to exactly one brief.

### Linking Rules

1. **Every spoke links back to its territory pillar anchor** — this is the strongest internal link signal within a territory
2. **Every spoke links to the pillar hub** — via in-content link and breadcrumb navigation
3. **Pillar anchors link to every spoke in their cluster** — anchors function as territory-level index pages
4. **Cross-territory bridges link to both territory pillar anchors** — connecting related concepts across territories
5. **Cross-pillar content links to both relevant pillar hubs** — when content genuinely spans pillars (e.g., testosterone + GLP-1)

9. **Anchor text varies** — do not use the same anchor text for the same destination across multiple articles. Use natural language anchors that match the context of the linking sentence.
10. **Service page CTAs are contextual, not interruptive** — they appear as natural recommendations within the content ("If these patterns match your experience, a clinical evaluation is a reasonable next step") rather than banner ads or pop-up modules.

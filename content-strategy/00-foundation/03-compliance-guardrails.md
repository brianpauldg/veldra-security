# Compliance Guardrails — Bloom Metabolics Content

---

## Overview

This document is the compliance reference for all Bloom Metabolics content. Every article, landing page, email, ad, and social post must conform to the language standards, disclaimers, and review processes defined here.

Non-compliance is not a style issue. It is a business risk. A single improperly worded claim can delay LegitScript certification, trigger FDA warning letters to our compounding partners, or expose the practice to FTC enforcement for deceptive health claims.

When in doubt, use more conservative language. When under time pressure, do not skip this document.

## 1. Approved Language vs Prohibited Language

### TRT Content Language

| Context | Approved Phrasing | Prohibited Phrasing | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Describing TRT outcomes | "Patients commonly report improvements in energy, libido, and body composition" | "TRT will restore your energy, sex drive, and help you lose fat" | Individual outcomes vary. "Will" implies guaranteed results. "Commonly report" reflects clinical observation without guaranteeing individual outcomes. |
| Referencing testosterone levels | "Testosterone levels that fall below the clinical threshold on two separate morning draws may indicate hypogonadism" | "If your T is low, you need TRT" | Clinical diagnosis requires specific criteria. "May indicate" reflects diagnostic complexity. "You need" implies a prescription decision before evaluation. |
| Discussing safety | "TRT has a documented safety profile when administered under clinical supervision with regular lab monitoring" | "TRT is completely safe" / "TRT has no serious side effects" | No medication is without risk. Blanket safety claims are indefensible and non-compliant. |
| Marketing TRT services | "Bloom offers clinician-supervised testosterone optimization with comprehensive lab monitoring" | "Get your testosterone prescribed online in minutes" | Speed-of-prescription framing undermines clinical credibility and may conflict with LegitScript standards for responsible prescribing. |
| Discussing fertility impact | "Exogenous testosterone may suppress spermatogenesis through HPG axis feedback inhibition. This effect is typically reversible upon discontinuation, though recovery timelines vary" | "TRT makes you infertile" / "TRT has no effect on fertility" | Both extremes are inaccurate. The actual clinical picture requires nuance about mechanism, reversibility, and individual variation. |
| Comparing to competitors | "Bloom's protocol includes comprehensive lab panels at regular intervals, which differs from platforms that offer less frequent monitoring" | "Other clinics don't care about your health" / "We're the only ones who do real medicine" | Disparaging competitors without substantiation is an FTC concern. Factual differentiation is permitted; subjective superiority claims are not. |
| Describing lab monitoring | "Our monitoring protocol includes total and free testosterone, SHBG, estradiol, hematocrit, PSA, lipid panel, and metabolic markers" | "We check everything" / "Complete panel" without specifying contents | Vague claims about monitoring comprehensiveness are unverifiable. Listing specific markers is both more compliant and more clinically credible. |
| Addressing age-related decline | "Age-related decline in testosterone production is well-documented, though the rate and clinical significance vary considerably between individuals" | "Every man over 30 is losing testosterone and needs treatment" | Overgeneralizing age-related decline to imply universal treatment need is a disease-mongering pattern that regulators and LegitScript reviewers flag. |

### Peptide Content Language

| Context | Approved Phrasing | Prohibited Phrasing | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Discussing peptide research | "Preclinical research on [peptide] has demonstrated [specific finding] in [animal model/in vitro setting]" | "This peptide does X" / "[Peptide] is proven to..." | Most peptide evidence is preclinical. Stating effects as established clinical fact when the evidence is preclinical is scientifically inaccurate and potentially actionable as a deceptive health claim. |
| Discussing availability | "The availability of [peptide] for compounding is subject to FDA categorization, which is reviewed periodically" | "You can still get [peptide]" / "We offer [peptide] therapy" | Bloom does not currently offer peptide therapy as a clinical service. Peptide content is educational only. Implying clinical availability is non-compliant with Bloom's current service scope. |
| Discussing regulatory status | "As of [date], [peptide] is classified as [Category 1/2/3] under the FDA's bulk drug substance evaluation process" | "Legal" / "Illegal" / "Banned" | FDA peptide regulation operates through a categorization system, not a legal/illegal binary. Using "banned" or "illegal" is inaccurate. Using "legal" implies broader permissibility than the categorization system provides. |
| Discussing clinical applications | "Researchers are investigating [peptide]'s potential applications in [area], though clinical trial data in human subjects remains limited" | "This peptide treats [condition]" / "[Peptide] is used for [therapeutic claim]" | Treatment claims require FDA-approved indications. Peptide content must frame research applications, not treatment claims. |
| Referencing consumer interest | "Consumer interest in peptide therapy has increased substantially, driven in part by online communities and social media discussion" | "Everyone is using peptides" / "Peptides are the future of medicine" | Normalizing unregulated use or making predictive claims about peptide medicine is neither scientifically supported nor compliant. |
| Discussing safety | "The safety profile of [peptide] in human subjects has not been established through large-scale clinical trials. Available safety data is derived primarily from preclinical models and small-scale observational reports" | "Peptides are natural and safe" / "No known side effects" | Safety cannot be established without adequate human trial data. "Natural" has no regulatory meaning and does not imply safety. |

## 2. FDA Compounding Rules Summary

### 503A vs 503B Distinction

The Drug Quality and Security Act (DQSA), enacted in 2013, established two categories of compounding:

**503A Compounding Pharmacies:**
- Compound medications based on individual patient prescriptions
- Must have a valid prescriber-patient relationship
- Subject to state pharmacy board oversight
- Not required to register with FDA as outsourcing facilities
- Cannot compound in anticipation of receiving prescriptions (no "office stock")
- Must use bulk drug substances that are components of FDA-approved drugs, appear on the FDA's list of bulk drug substances that can be used in compounding, or are the subject of an applicable USP or NF monograph
- Bloom's compounding pharmacy partner operates under 503A

**503B Outsourcing Facilities:**
- May compound without individual prescriptions
- Must register with FDA
- Subject to FDA cGMP requirements and inspection
- May produce larger quantities (closer to manufacturing)
- Higher regulatory burden but broader distribution permitted

**Key operational note:** Bloom's clinical model relies on 503A compounding, which requires a valid prescriber-patient relationship for every prescription. This means content cannot promote "order online" or "get medication shipped" framing that implies a direct-to-consumer product model without clinical evaluation.

### GLP-1 Compounding Conditions

Compounding of semaglutide by 503A pharmacies has been permitted under specific conditions:

1. **Drug shortage status:** When semaglutide appeared on the FDA drug shortage list, 503A pharmacies could compound it under the shortage exception. The shortage status has fluctuated — tirzepatide was removed from the shortage list in late 2024, and semaglutide's status has been subject to ongoing review.

2. **Active ingredient sourcing:** The compounding pharmacy must source the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) from a registered facility. Supply chain integrity is a compliance concern.

3. **"Essentially a copy" rule:** Under 503A, pharmacies generally may not compound a drug that is "essentially a copy" of a commercially available drug. However, compounding during a shortage is a recognized exception. When a shortage resolves, the legal basis for compounding may narrow. Content must reflect the current regulatory status and should include a "current as of" date.

4. **Salt form distinction:** Some compounding pharmacies use semaglutide sodium (a salt form) rather than the base form used in branded products. The regulatory significance of this distinction is actively being litigated and reviewed. Content should not take a position on the salt form debate but should note that it is an evolving area.

**Content implication:** All GLP-1 content that references compounding must include a clear disclosure that compounded medications are not FDA-approved products. The regulatory basis for compounding must be accurately stated as of the publication date.

### Peptide Category Statuses

The FDA evaluates bulk drug substances for compounding eligibility through a categorization process:

**Category 1 — Eligible for compounding:**
Substances that have been evaluated and may be used by compounding pharmacies. These substances meet the FDA's criteria for safety and have a sufficient basis for compounding use.

**Category 2 — Under review:**
Substances for which the FDA has not yet completed its evaluation. These may or may not ultimately be approved for compounding. Availability during the review period varies.

**Category 3 — Not eligible for compounding:**
Substances that the FDA has determined do not meet the criteria for compounding use, or that present safety concerns that preclude compounding. 503A pharmacies may not compound Category 3 substances.

**Content implication:** Peptide articles must reference the specific category of each peptide discussed and note the date of that categorization. Categories can change as FDA review progresses. Articles must be updated when categories change.

### April 2026 Enforcement Actions

In April 2026, the FDA issued enforcement actions targeting compounding pharmacies and telehealth platforms that were marketing compounded peptides without adequate regulatory basis. Specific actions included:

- Warning letters to pharmacies compounding Category 3 substances
- Cease-and-desist communications to telehealth platforms making therapeutic claims about non-approved peptides
- Increased scrutiny of marketing language that implied FDA approval of compounded products

**Content implication:** These enforcement actions reinforce the need for precise language in all peptide and compounding content. Bloom must not be positioned as offering, endorsing, or facilitating access to peptides that are not currently eligible for compounding. All peptide content must be framed as educational.

### "Essentially a Copy" Rule

Section 503A of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act generally prohibits 503A pharmacies from compounding drugs that are "essentially a copy" of a commercially available drug. A compounded drug is considered essentially a copy if it is identical or nearly identical to an approved drug in terms of active ingredient, route of administration, dosage form, and strength.

Key exceptions:
- The prescriber determines that a change to the commercially available product (e.g., different strength, removal of an allergen) produces a clinical difference for the individual patient
- The drug is on the FDA drug shortage list

**Content implication:** Bloom content must not describe compounded medications as copies, alternatives, or versions of branded products. The clinical rationale for compounding (patient-specific needs, shortage access) must be framed correctly.

## 4. LegitScript-Specific Considerations

Bloom Metabolics has a LegitScript application in progress. LegitScript certification is required for advertising on Google Ads, Meta (Facebook/Instagram), and other major platforms for healthcare and pharmacy services.

LegitScript evaluates:

1. **Legitimacy of the prescribing model.** Content must reflect a genuine clinical evaluation process — not a "questionnaire-to-prescription" pipeline. The consultation ($49 Optimization Consultation) must be presented as a real clinical encounter, not a formality.

2. **Accuracy of health claims.** All claims must be evidence-based and properly qualified. LegitScript reviewers specifically flag:
   - Guaranteed outcome language
   - "Before and after" imagery without proper disclaimers
   - Claims of FDA approval for non-approved products
   - Minimization of side effects or risks

3. **Prescribing practices.** Content must not suggest that prescriptions are issued without adequate clinical evaluation, including appropriate lab work and medical history review.

4. **Compounding disclosures.** LegitScript requires clear disclosure that compounded medications are not FDA-approved. The GLP-1 compounded medication disclosure (above) satisfies this requirement.

5. **Pharmacy partner transparency.** While Bloom must not publicly name its compounding pharmacy partner, content must not obscure the fact that medications are compounded. "Prescription medications delivered to your door" without compounding disclosure is insufficient.

6. **Website compliance.** LegitScript reviews the entire website, not just the application materials. Non-compliant content anywhere on the site can delay or deny certification. This includes blog posts, social media embeds, and any patient-facing text.

**Action items for content:**
- Ensure every page with medication discussion includes the appropriate disclaimer
- Remove any language suggesting prescriptions are guaranteed or automatic
- Ensure the consultation is described as a clinical evaluation, not a sales step
- Never use "doctor-approved" as a marketing claim — it implies blanket endorsement

## 5. HIPAA Considerations for Content

Bloom Metabolics is subject to HIPAA as a covered entity providing healthcare services. Content that references patient experiences must comply with HIPAA privacy requirements.

### Rules for Content

1. **Never reference identifiable patient information** in any content, including blog posts, case studies, social media, or marketing materials, without a signed HIPAA authorization from the patient.

2. **De-identification standards.** If patient experiences are used in content (even with authorization), they must be de-identified per the HIPAA Safe Harbor method:
   - Remove all 18 HIPAA identifiers (names, dates, locations, ages over 89, etc.)
   - Or obtain expert determination that re-identification risk is very small

3. **Composite cases.** When illustrating clinical scenarios, use clearly labeled composite or hypothetical cases rather than actual patient stories. Label these explicitly: "The following is a hypothetical clinical scenario for educational purposes."

4. **Internal communications.** Content drafts that reference real patient scenarios for inspiration must not include PHI. If clinical details are used to inform content, they must be fully de-identified before entering the content workflow.

5. **Screenshots and images.** Never include screenshots of patient portals, lab results, or clinical interfaces in marketing content, even with names redacted, unless the content has been reviewed for all 18 identifiers.

## 6. How to Handle Testimonials and Case Studies

Bloom does not currently publish patient testimonials or case studies. When these are introduced, the following protocols apply.

### Testimonial Collection Protocol

1. **Written authorization.** Every patient providing a testimonial must sign a separate HIPAA authorization specifically for marketing use. This authorization must specify:
   - What information will be disclosed
   - How and where it will be used
   - The patient's right to revoke authorization at any time

2. **No incentive disclosure.** If any incentive is provided for a testimonial (discount, free month, etc.), this must be disclosed alongside the testimonial per FTC endorsement guidelines.

3. **Accuracy review.** Every testimonial must be reviewed for factual accuracy. Patients may not claim outcomes that are not documented in their clinical record (if such claims are specific and verifiable).

4. **Disclaimer placement.** The testimonial disclaimer (Section 3 above) must appear in close proximity to any testimonial — not buried in a footer on a different page.

### Case Study Protocol

1. **Full de-identification.** Case studies must be fully de-identified per HIPAA Safe Harbor standards.

2. **Clinical accuracy.** Case study content must be reviewed by the medical reviewer to confirm clinical accuracy of the described scenario.

3. **"Results not typical" framing.** Case studies must include language clarifying that the described outcome is an individual case and should not be interpreted as a typical or expected result.

4. **No before/after imagery.** Before-and-after photos are high-risk for compliance. If used, they require:
   - Written HIPAA authorization
   - Testimonial disclaimer
   - "Results not typical" language
   - No digital alteration
   - Consistent lighting, angle, and framing
   - LegitScript review before publication

## 7. Pre-Publish Compliance Checklist

Before any content is published, confirm every item:

- [ ] **1. No absolute health claims.** Every outcome statement uses qualifying language ("may," "commonly reported," "clinical data suggests"). No "will," "guaranteed," or "proven."

- [ ] **2. No named pharmacy partner.** The compounding pharmacy is never identified by name in patient-facing content. References use "our licensed compounding pharmacy partner" or equivalent.

- [ ] **3. Compounding disclosure present.** Any page discussing compounded medications includes the GLP-1 Compounded Medication Disclosure (or equivalent for non-GLP-1 compounded medications).

- [ ] **4. Peptide content framed as educational.** Peptide articles do not include service page CTAs, do not imply clinical availability, and include the Peptide Educational-Only Disclosure.

- [ ] **5. Standard medical disclaimer present.** The footer disclaimer appears on every page.

- [ ] **6. No brand-name conflation.** Compounded semaglutide is not described as Ozempic, Wegovy, or equivalent. Brand names are used only for educational context with clear distinction.

- [ ] **7. Clinical evaluation framing.** The consultation is described as a clinical evaluation, not a purchase step or formality.

- [ ] **8. Side effects addressed honestly.** Content discussing medications includes accurate side effect information with frequency data where available. No minimization.

- [ ] **9. No prohibited language.** Content reviewed against the forbidden words and phrases list in `01-positioning-and-voice.md` and the approved/prohibited language tables above.

- [ ] **10. Citations present for medical claims.** Every medical claim links to or references a primary source (peer-reviewed journal, FDA guidance, clinical guideline).

- [ ] **11. Medical reviewer signoff.** The designated medical reviewer has reviewed and approved the content. Reviewer name and credentials appear in the article attribution.

- [ ] **12. No identifiable patient information.** Content does not contain PHI or identifiable patient information. Any patient scenarios are clearly labeled as hypothetical or composite.

- [ ] **13. Phentermine not referenced.** No mention of phentermine in any context. Phentermine is permanently excluded from Bloom's content and service offerings (see Section 8).

- [ ] **14. "Last reviewed" date present.** Articles display the date of the most recent medical review.

- [ ] **15. Regulatory status current.** Any content referencing regulatory status (compounding availability, peptide categories, drug shortage status) reflects the current regulatory landscape as of the publication date, with a "current as of" notation.

## 8. Phentermine Exclusion Notice

Phentermine is permanently excluded from all Bloom Metabolics content, marketing, and clinical services.

**This exclusion is absolute.** Do not reference phentermine in:
- Blog posts or educational articles
- Landing pages or service descriptions
- Email sequences or nurture campaigns
- Social media content
- Paid advertising
- Internal content briefs
- Comparison articles (even as a "we don't offer this" reference)
- FAQ pages
- Patient communications

**Rationale:** Phentermine does not align with Bloom's clinical philosophy, brand positioning, or long-term regulatory strategy. The decision to exclude phentermine is final and not subject to periodic review.

**If asked about phentermine** by a patient or prospective patient, the response is: "Bloom Metabolics does not offer phentermine-based therapies. Our metabolic health protocols focus on GLP-1 receptor agonist therapy combined with lifestyle and body composition management."

**Content review note:** If phentermine appears in any draft content — including content generated by AI tools — it must be removed before publication. This is a standing editorial rule that does not expire.

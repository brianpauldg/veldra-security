# E-E-A-T and Medical Review Strategy — Bloom Metabolics

---

## 1. What E-E-A-T Is and Why It Dominates Medical SEO

E-E-A-T stands for Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. It is the framework Google's Search Quality Raters use to evaluate content quality, and it directly influences how Google's algorithms rank pages — particularly for YMYL (Your Money or Your Life) queries.

YMYL content is any content that could significantly impact a person's health, financial stability, or safety. Medical content — which is the entirety of Bloom's /learn/ subdirectory — is the most scrutinized YMYL category.

For YMYL medical content, the practical implications are:

- **Experience:** The content creator has real-world experience with the subject matter. For Bloom, this means clinical experience — not "I tried TRT and here's what happened" but "I have managed TRT protocols for patients and understand the clinical considerations."

- **Expertise:** The content creator has formal credentials relevant to the topic. An RN writing about TRT protocols has demonstrable expertise. A freelance health writer without clinical credentials does not, regardless of writing quality.

- **Authoritativeness:** The website and its creators are recognized as authoritative sources. This is built over time through backlinks from reputable sites, citations in other content, consistent publication of high-quality material, and visible institutional credibility (medical reviewer attribution, professional affiliations).

- **Trustworthiness:** The content is accurate, transparent about its sources, honest about limitations, and clearly identifies who created and reviewed it. For medical content, trust requires visible clinical oversight — not just a disclaimer that "this is not medical advice."

### Why This Matters for Bloom Specifically

Bloom competes against platforms with larger domains, higher domain authority, and more content volume. In traditional SEO terms, Bloom starts at a disadvantage. E-E-A-T is the equalizer.

Google's algorithms increasingly favor content that demonstrates genuine clinical expertise over content that is well-optimized but author-less. A 2,500-word article on free testosterone written by a credentialed clinician with a visible medical reviewer, proper citations, and a "last reviewed" date will outperform a 5,000-word article from a content mill with no attributed author — even if the content mill has higher domain authority.

Every structural decision in Bloom's content strategy — the author bio blocks, the medical reviewer attribution, the citation requirements, the schema markup — is designed to maximize E-E-A-T signals for Google's quality evaluation systems.

## 2. Bloom's Credentialing Display Strategy

E-E-A-T is not just about having credentials. It is about making those credentials visible, verifiable, and consistently displayed across every piece of content.

### Founder/Author Bio Block

Every article written or substantially developed by Brian displays the following bio block. This block appears:
- Below the article title and publication date
- Above the fold (visible without scrolling)
- Adjacent to the "Medically reviewed by" attribution

The bio block includes:
- Author name with credentials (Brian DeGuzman, RN)
- Headshot (professional, consistent across all appearances)
- Brief bio (see sample below)
- Link to the full author bio page (/about/brian-deguzman)

The author bio page is a standalone page with:
- Full professional biography (250-400 words)
- Education and credentials
- Clinical experience summary
- Professional affiliations
- Link to professional license verification (where available)
- Structured data (Person schema, see Section 8)

### Medical Reviewer Block

Every article displays the medical reviewer attribution. This block appears:
- Directly adjacent to the author bio block
- Includes reviewer name with credentials
- Includes the date of the most recent review
- Links to the reviewer's full bio page

The reviewer bio page follows the same structure as the author bio page.

### Organization Credentials

The Bloom Metabolics website displays organizational credentials in persistent site elements (footer or about section):
- Practice type (telehealth, cash-pay)
- Location (Irvine, CA)
- LegitScript certification status (once obtained)
- State licensing information
- Professional affiliations (as applicable)

These organizational credentials are encoded in the MedicalOrganization schema markup (see `04-technical-seo-checklist.md`).

### Credential Consistency Rule

The same credentials must be displayed the same way across every occurrence. Do not abbreviate "Registered Nurse" in one place and spell it out in another. Do not list "RN" in an article byline and "R.N." on the about page. Establish one format and enforce it everywhere.

Bloom's standard formats:
- Brian DeGuzman, RN (no periods in credential abbreviation)
- Dr. Michael Napolitano, MD

## 5. Review and Update Cadence Policy

Medical content degrades. Clinical guidelines change. Studies are published that alter the evidence landscape. FDA regulations shift. A "last reviewed" date that is 18 months old actively damages E-E-A-T signals.

### Cadence Rules

| Content Type | Initial Review | Subsequent Review Cadence | Trigger for Off-Cycle Review |
|---|---|---|---|
| TRT spoke articles | Before publication | Every 12 months | New clinical guideline (Endocrine Society, AUA), major study publication (comparable to TRAVERSE), FDA labeling change |
| GLP-1 spoke articles | Before publication | Every 6 months | FDA compounding guidance change, drug shortage list update, new trial data (STEP, SURMOUNT series), regulatory enforcement action |
| Peptide spoke articles | Before publication | Every 3-6 months | FDA category reclassification, enforcement action, significant preclinical publication |
| Pillar hub pages | Before publication | Every 6 months | Any spoke article update that changes the pillar's scope or structure |
| Service pages | Before publication | Every 6 months | Pricing change, service scope change, regulatory change |

### "Last Reviewed" Date Discipline

- Every article displays: "Last medically reviewed on [Month Day, Year] by [Reviewer Name, Credentials]"
- This date is updated ONLY when a substantive review occurs — not when a typo is fixed or a link is updated
- A substantive review means the reviewer has re-read the article, confirmed that the clinical claims remain accurate, and verified that no new evidence has materially changed the article's conclusions
- The "Last medically reviewed" date must be visible above the fold, near the article title
- The date must also be reflected in the `lastReviewed` field of the MedicalWebPage schema and the `dateModified` field of the Article schema

### Review Process

1. **Scheduled review:** On the cadence schedule above, the article enters the review queue. The current published version is sent to the medical reviewer.

2. **Reviewer assessment:** The reviewer evaluates whether the content remains accurate. They flag any claims that need updating, any new evidence that should be incorporated, and any language that no longer reflects current clinical practice.

3. **Content update:** If changes are needed, the article is updated. If no changes are needed, the "Last medically reviewed" date is still updated to reflect the new review.

4. **Publication:** The updated article is deployed. The schema markup dates are updated. The sitemap `<lastmod>` is updated.

5. **Documentation:** The review is logged — which article, which reviewer, what date, what changes (if any). This log serves as an audit trail for compliance and LegitScript review.

## 6. Citation Hierarchy

Not all sources are equal. Bloom content follows a strict hierarchy for citation quality, and higher-quality sources are always preferred.

### Tier 1: Peer-Reviewed Journal Articles (PubMed-Indexed)

This is the gold standard. Randomized controlled trials, systematic reviews, and meta-analyses published in peer-reviewed journals are the strongest citation type.

**Preferred journals for Bloom's content areas:**
- The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism
- The New England Journal of Medicine
- The Lancet
- JAMA (Journal of the American Medical Association)
- Obesity
- Diabetes Care
- The Journal of Urology
- Fertility and Sterility
- Endocrine Reviews

**Citation format:** Link to the PubMed entry or the journal's DOI-resolved page. Include the authors, title, journal, year, and PMID in the "Sources" section at the article's end.

### Tier 2: FDA and Clinical Practice Guidelines

FDA guidance documents, FDA-approved drug labeling (package inserts), and clinical practice guidelines from recognized professional organizations.

**Key organizations:**
- FDA (drug labeling, guidance documents, safety communications)
- Endocrine Society (clinical practice guidelines for testosterone therapy)
- American Urological Association (guidelines for testosterone deficiency)
- American Diabetes Association (standards of medical care)
- Obesity Medicine Association

**Citation format:** Link directly to the FDA or organization's published document. Include the document title, issuing organization, and date.

### Tier 3: Major Academic Medical Center Publications

Educational content from major academic medical centers, when the content is reviewed by their clinical faculty.

**Accepted sources:**
- Mayo Clinic
- Cleveland Clinic
- Johns Hopkins Medicine
- Harvard Health
- Stanford Medicine
- UCSF Health

**Use case:** These sources are acceptable for general explanations, patient-facing definitions, and context — but not as the primary citation for specific clinical claims. If a clinical claim can be cited to a Tier 1 source, it should be.

### Tier 4: Established Medical Textbooks

Endocrinology, pharmacology, and physiology textbooks with named authors and established editorial reputations.

**Use case:** Foundational explanations of physiology or pharmacology that are not specific to a single study. For example, explaining the HPG axis using a textbook reference is appropriate. Citing a specific clinical outcome from a textbook rather than the underlying study is not.

### Sources That Must Not Be Cited

- News articles about studies (cite the study, not the news coverage)
- WebMD, Healthline, or similar consumer health aggregators (these are competitors, not sources)
- Supplement company white papers or sponsored research
- Podcast episodes, YouTube videos, or social media posts
- Wikipedia
- Competitor telehealth websites
- Preprint servers (bioRxiv, medRxiv) — these are acceptable to mention as emerging evidence but must be labeled as "not yet peer-reviewed"
- Animal studies cited as if they are human evidence (animal studies may be cited but must be clearly labeled as preclinical)

## 7. The "Show Your Work" Principle

E-E-A-T for medical content is not just about having the right credentials — it is about making the clinical reasoning visible to the reader.

A page that says "TRT is safe when monitored" demonstrates less expertise than a page that explains: "The primary hematologic risk of exogenous testosterone is erythrocytosis — an increase in red blood cell mass that elevates hematocrit. When hematocrit exceeds 54%, the risk of thromboembolic events increases. This is why a hematocrit check is included in every monitoring panel, typically at 8-week intervals during dose titration. The risk is manageable precisely because it is predictable and measurable."

The second version shows the work. It demonstrates that the author understands the mechanism, the monitoring protocol, and the clinical reasoning behind both. Google's quality raters — and more importantly, Bloom's readers — can distinguish between these two approaches.

### How to Show Your Work in Bloom Content

1. **Name the mechanism.** Do not say "TRT may affect red blood cell production." Say "Exogenous testosterone stimulates erythropoiesis via erythropoietin upregulation, which can elevate hematocrit."

2. **Quantify when possible.** Do not say "GLP-1 medications cause significant weight loss." Say "In the STEP 1 trial, participants receiving semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly lost a mean of 14.9% of body weight over 68 weeks, compared to 2.4% in the placebo group."

3. **Name the study.** When citing evidence, name the trial or study by name (TRAVERSE, STEP 1, SURMOUNT-1) rather than saying "studies show" or "research indicates." Named references are more credible and more verifiable.

4. **Acknowledge limitations.** Show expertise by noting what the evidence does not show, where gaps exist, and where clinical judgment fills in where data is limited. This is the hallmark of genuine clinical reasoning — a willingness to say "we do not know this yet."

5. **Connect mechanism to recommendation.** Every recommendation should trace back to a mechanism or evidence base. The reader should feel that the recommendation follows logically from the explanation — not that it was stated as authority.

6. **Distinguish evidence quality.** A systematic review of 15 RCTs is stronger evidence than a single case series. Preclinical evidence is weaker than clinical evidence. Make these distinctions visible. "Preclinical data in murine models suggests..." is more credible than "research shows..." because it demonstrates that the author knows the difference.

### Linking Author and Reviewer Schema to Articles

Every article's MedicalWebPage schema (defined in `04-technical-seo-checklist.md`) references the author and reviewer via `@id` or direct nesting. The `author` and `reviewedBy` fields in the article schema should use `url` values that match the `url` in the Person schema on the bio pages. This creates a connected graph of structured data that Google can traverse:

Article schema → `author.url` → Author bio page → Person schema
Article schema → `reviewedBy.url` → Reviewer bio page → Person schema

This connection establishes that the article was written by a specific credentialed person and reviewed by a specific credentialed physician. It is the structured data equivalent of the visible bio blocks on the article page.

### Schema Validation

After implementing Person schemas on bio pages and article schemas on content pages:

1. Test each page with Google's Rich Results Test (https://search.google.com/test/rich-results)
2. Verify that the Person entities are detected and their properties are correctly parsed
3. Verify that the MedicalWebPage schema's `author` and `reviewedBy` fields resolve correctly
4. Check the Schema Markup Validator (https://validator.schema.org/) for syntax errors
5. Monitor Google Search Console's Enhancements reports for structured data errors after publication

### How Concept Territory Ownership Compounds E-E-A-T Over Time

E-E-A-T is not a one-time achievement — it compounds. Bloom's concept territory framework is designed to accelerate this compounding effect.

**The mechanism:** When Bloom publishes one article on "the symptomatic threshold," Google evaluates that article on its own merits. When Bloom publishes a pillar anchor, three primary spokes, and two cross-territory bridges that all reference "the symptomatic threshold" with consistent clinical vocabulary, Google evaluates Bloom as a topical authority on the phenomenon. The whole becomes greater than the sum of the parts.

**How territories compound E-E-A-T:**
1. **Expertise signal amplification.** Each new article in a territory cluster reinforces the expertise demonstrated in every other article. The clinical vocabulary overlap (SHBG, free testosterone, Leydig cells, gonadotropin) creates a semantic footprint that Google's neural matching associates with genuine endocrine expertise.
2. **Authoritativeness through comprehensive coverage.** A single article on testosterone and fertility establishes some authority. A pillar anchor on Fertility-Preserving TRT with a spoke on HCG protocols, a cross-link to the comprehensive TRT guide, and consistent terminology across all pieces establishes territorial authority. Competitors would need to replicate the entire cluster, not just match a single article.
3. **Trustworthiness through consistency.** The same named concepts, the same evidence-tier framework, the same compliance language, the same reviewer attribution — this consistency is itself a trust signal. It demonstrates institutional rigor, not one-off article production.

**Timeline for compounding:**
- Months 1-3: Individual articles begin ranking. Territory clusters are incomplete but pillar anchors establish initial positions.
- Months 3-6: Full territory clusters are published. Internal linking creates semantic connections. Google begins evaluating cluster authority, not just page authority.
- Months 6-12: Compounding takes effect. Bloom's territory pages begin outranking higher-DA competitors on territory-specific queries because the topical depth signal exceeds the domain authority signal.

## 3. The Five-Element Content Template

Every Bloom Metabolics article follows five structural elements. These are not optional. They are the architecture that distinguishes Bloom content from commodity health writing.

### Element 1: Clinical Opening

The first paragraph leads with the mechanism or the clinical question — never with a hook, a statistic pulled from a press release, or a "Did you know?" framing. The reader should feel like they have opened a clinical briefing, not a blog post.

The opening should pose the question the article answers or state the physiological mechanism the article explains. It should do this in two to four sentences. No preamble. No "In today's world of..." throat-clearing.

**Example:** "Semaglutide and tirzepatide activate overlapping but pharmacologically distinct receptor pathways. The clinical differences between these two GLP-1 receptor agonists — in appetite suppression magnitude, glycemic control, and lean mass impact — follow directly from that receptor-level distinction. Understanding the mechanism clarifies the protocol decision."

### Element 2: Mechanism Before Recommendation

Before the article tells the reader what to do, it explains why. This is the structural principle that separates Bloom content from content that simply lists recommendations.

If an article recommends monitoring free testosterone rather than total testosterone alone, it first explains SHBG binding, albumin-bound fractions, and the bioavailable testosterone concept. The recommendation follows from the explanation. The reader should feel that the recommendation is inevitable given the mechanism — not that it was handed down as authority.

Every recommendation in a Bloom article must be preceded by its mechanistic rationale. If you cannot explain why, do not include the recommendation.

### Element 3: Contrarian Angle

Every article contains at least one moment where Bloom pushes back against conventional wisdom, popular belief, or oversimplified industry narratives. This is not contrarianism for engagement — it is clinical precision applied to areas where the popular understanding is incomplete.

This might look like:
- Explaining why a "normal" testosterone level may still warrant clinical attention
- Challenging the assumption that GLP-1 therapy is purely about appetite suppression
- Noting that "natural testosterone boosters" have a specific evidentiary gap that supplement marketing obscures

The contrarian angle must always be evidence-backed. It is never a hot take. It is a correction.

### Element 4: Cited Primary Sources

Every medical claim in a Bloom article must be backed by a cited source. The citation hierarchy is:

1. Peer-reviewed journal articles (PubMed-indexed)
2. FDA guidance documents, clinical practice guidelines (Endocrine Society, AUA, etc.)
3. Major academic medical center publications (Mayo, Cleveland Clinic, Johns Hopkins)
4. Established medical textbooks (endocrinology, pharmacology)

Do not cite:
- News articles about studies (cite the study directly)
- Supplement company white papers
- Podcast episodes or YouTube videos
- Wikipedia
- Other telehealth competitor content

Citations should be inline with the claim, formatted as hyperlinks to the source. At the bottom of the article, include a "Sources" section with full references.

### Element 5: Reviewer Attribution

Every article displays the medical reviewer's name, credentials, and a brief bio above the fold. This is not a footer disclaimer — it is a visible trust signal positioned near the article title and publication date.

The reviewer attribution block includes:
- "Medically reviewed by [Name], [Credentials]"
- Date of review
- A link to the reviewer's full bio page

This element is non-negotiable for YMYL content. It is the single most important E-E-A-T signal on the page.

## 4. Voice Rules — Bloom vs Generic Telehealth

### Example 1: Describing Low Testosterone Symptoms

**Generic telehealth content:**
"Feeling tired all the time? Low energy, brain fog, and decreased sex drive could be signs of low testosterone. Millions of men suffer from Low T without even knowing it. Take our free quiz to find out if you might have low testosterone and discover how TRT could help you feel like yourself again."

**Bloom voice:**
"Fatigue that persists despite adequate sleep, cognitive slowing that was not present two years ago, and a decline in libido that tracks with no relational explanation — these are patterns, not symptoms in isolation. When they cluster in a man between 30 and 55, they warrant a lab panel, not a quiz. Total testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, and a metabolic panel establish whether the pattern has a hormonal substrate. Many men presenting with these complaints have total testosterone within the reference range but suppressed free testosterone due to elevated SHBG — a distinction most screening approaches miss entirely."

---

### Example 2: Explaining GLP-1 Weight Loss

**Generic telehealth content:**
"GLP-1 medications like semaglutide and tirzepatide are revolutionizing weight loss. These powerful drugs can help you lose up to 15-20% of your body weight. Our doctors will create a personalized plan to help you reach your weight loss goals faster than diet and exercise alone. Start your transformation today."

**Bloom voice:**
"GLP-1 receptor agonists reduce caloric intake through two converging mechanisms: delayed gastric emptying and central appetite suppression via hypothalamic signaling. The weight loss observed in clinical trials — approximately 15% of body weight with semaglutide, approximately 21% with tirzepatide at maximum doses — represents a pharmacological effect, not a metabolic correction. This distinction matters clinically. Approximately 30-40% of weight lost on GLP-1 monotherapy is lean mass, which carries long-term implications for metabolic rate, functional capacity, and body composition quality. A protocol that pairs GLP-1 therapy with resistance training and adequate protein intake addresses a problem that the medication alone does not solve."

### Example 3: Discussing TRT Cost

**Generic telehealth content:**
"Worried about the cost of TRT? At [Brand], we believe everyone deserves access to hormone therapy. Our affordable plans start at just $X/month with everything included — medication, labs, and provider visits. No hidden fees, no insurance headaches. See how easy and affordable it is to get started."

**Bloom voice:**
"TRT cost structures in cash-pay telehealth vary substantially, and the variation is not primarily in the testosterone itself. Injectable testosterone cypionate is inexpensive — the raw medication cost, whether compounded or commercially manufactured, is a small fraction of what most platforms charge. The meaningful cost differences lie in what surrounds the prescription: the frequency and scope of lab monitoring, the clinical time allocated to protocol adjustment, and whether ancillary medications (HCG, anastrozole, DHEA) are included or billed separately. A platform quoting $99/month with quarterly labs and a platform quoting $299/month with comprehensive panels every eight weeks are not offering the same clinical product, even if the testosterone vial is identical."

### Example 4: Addressing TRT Safety Concerns

**Generic telehealth content:**
"Is TRT safe? The short answer is yes — when prescribed and monitored by a qualified doctor, testosterone replacement therapy is safe and effective. Side effects are rare and usually manageable. Don't let myths about TRT hold you back from feeling your best. Our expert medical team will monitor your health every step of the way."

**Bloom voice:**
"The safety profile of exogenous testosterone administration depends on variables that a binary 'safe or unsafe' framing obscures. Route of administration, dosing frequency, starting dose relative to baseline levels, concurrent use of aromatase inhibitors, and the adequacy of hematologic monitoring all modulate risk. The most commonly cited concern — cardiovascular risk — was shaped by a 2010 study (Basaria et al.) with significant methodological limitations, including an older, higher-comorbidity population than the typical TRT candidate. Subsequent data, including the TRAVERSE trial (2023), provided more reassuring cardiovascular signal in hypogonadal men without pre-existing cardiovascular disease. What remains non-negotiable is monitoring: hematocrit, PSA, lipid panels, and estradiol should be tracked at regular intervals. TRT without structured monitoring is not TRT — it is self-administration with a prescription attached."

**Bloom voice:**

## 6. Sentence Rhythm and Structural Notes

### The Declarative-Then-Elaborate Pattern

Bloom content follows a specific rhythmic structure. Short declarative sentences establish the point. Longer sentences that follow provide the clinical elaboration, the mechanism, the nuance.

This is not a stylistic preference. It is a readability architecture. The short sentence anchors the reader. The longer sentence teaches. Together they create a rhythm that feels authoritative without being dense and accessible without being shallow.

**Example of the pattern in action:**

"Free testosterone is the clinically actionable number. Total testosterone — the value most primary care labs report — includes testosterone bound to sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) and albumin, neither of which is bioavailable at the receptor level. A man with a total testosterone of 500 ng/dL and elevated SHBG may have free testosterone in the bottom quintile of the reference range. His symptoms are explained. His lab report says he is normal."

Notice: Short sentence. Long sentence. Medium sentence. Short sentence. Short sentence. The last two short sentences land with weight because the preceding longer sentences set up the clinical reasoning.

### Paragraph Length

Bloom paragraphs are typically 3-6 sentences. Single-sentence paragraphs are permitted for emphasis but should not appear more than once or twice per article section. Paragraphs longer than 8 sentences should be broken.

### Section Structure

Each major section of an article (H2 level) should follow this internal structure:

1. **Opening declarative** — states the section's central point in one or two sentences
2. **Mechanism or context** — explains the why behind the point (2-4 sentences)
3. **Evidence or specifics** — provides data, reference ranges, study results, or clinical detail (2-4 sentences)
4. **Clinical implication** — connects back to the patient's experience or decision (1-2 sentences)

Not every section will follow this exactly, but the pattern should be recognizable across the article.

### Transition Between Sections

Do not use transitional phrases like "Now let's look at..." or "Moving on to..." or "Another important factor is..." Sections should begin with their own declarative statement. The reader does not need a tour guide. The heading and the opening sentence orient them.

### Punctuation and Formatting

- No exclamation points. None. This is a clinical voice.
- Em dashes are permitted for parenthetical emphasis — used sparingly.
- Colons introduce lists or elaborations. They should not be overused.
- Bold text is used for key terms on first introduction, not for emphasis in running prose.
- Bullet lists are appropriate for reference material (lab ranges, medication options, protocol steps) but should not replace paragraph-form clinical reasoning.
- Numbers under 10 are written out in running prose. Numbers 10 and above use numerals. Lab values and dosages always use numerals.

### Tone Calibration

The voice is confident but not arrogant. It assumes the reader is intelligent but does not assume prior clinical knowledge. It explains without condescending. It recommends without pressuring.

If a sentence sounds like it belongs in a supplement ad, rewrite it. If a sentence sounds like it belongs in a medical journal abstract, add one clarifying sentence after it. The target register sits between clinical literature and a well-read patient newsletter.

The reader should finish a Bloom article feeling that they understand something they did not understand before — and that the source they learned it from has no reason to mislead them.

## 8. The Bloom Evidence Tier System

Every peptide monograph in the Bloom Peptide Library includes an evidence tier rating. The four tiers provide a standardized, honestly calibrated framework for rating evidence quality. This system is documented in full in the [PEP-EVIDENCE-TIERS brief](../02-briefs/peptides/PEP-EVIDENCE-TIERS.md) and is referenced across all monograph content.

### Tier 1 — Strong Human Clinical Evidence
Multiple independent human clinical trials (Phase II/III). Replicated across research groups and institutions. Published in peer-reviewed, PubMed-indexed journals. Clear mechanism of action with human pharmacokinetic data. Example: FDA-approved medications, established clinical interventions.

### Tier 2 — Established Mechanism with Limited Human Data
Well-characterized mechanism of action. Some human pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamic data exists. Preclinical evidence is strong and independently replicated. Clinical development may have begun but not completed Phase III. Examples: MOTS-c, GHK-Cu, CJC-1295, Ipamorelin.

### Tier 3 — Strong Preclinical, No Human Trials
Extensive animal model data, often from concentrated research groups. Mechanism is plausible and internally consistent. No completed human interventional trials. May have significant anecdotal patient reports (noted but not weighted as clinical evidence). Examples: BPC-157, TB-500.

### Tier 4 — Intriguing Hypothesis, Limited Validation
Research originates from limited sources (single lab, single country, single research tradition). Independent Western replication is sparse or absent. The hypothesis may be scientifically plausible but the evidence is insufficient for clinical confidence. May have regulatory approval in non-Western jurisdictions (noted as evidence of clinical use, not Western-standard validation). Examples: Epitalon, Selank.

### Application Rules
- Every monograph must include a tier rating with written justification
- Tier ratings are reviewed quarterly and may change as new evidence emerges
- A lower tier does not mean "worthless" — it means "less clinical confidence"
- A higher tier does not mean "safe and effective for you" — it means "more clinical data exists"
- Tier ratings do not replace clinical judgment or provider consultation

## 9. Concept Territory Discipline

Bloom Metabolics organizes all content around eight concept territories, each anchored by a named clinical concept. The full territory framework is documented in [06-concept-territories.md](06-concept-territories.md). This section defines the consistency requirement that applies across all content production.

### The Consistency Rule
Named concepts must appear with consistent phrasing across all cluster content. A concept is not a casual descriptor — it is a branded clinical term that Bloom owns through repeated, precise, and consistent usage across every article in the territory cluster.

### What This Means in Practice

**When a concept is introduced (pillar anchor article):**
- The named concept appears in the article title
- The concept is formally defined in the first H2 section
- The definition is substantive — mechanism, clinical relevance, why Bloom names it
- The concept appears throughout the article as a consistent defined term

**When a concept is referenced (spoke articles):**
- The named concept is referenced by name in the opening or first section
- The concept is not re-defined — it links back to the pillar anchor for the definition
- The concept's phrasing is identical to the pillar anchor (e.g., "Fertility-Preserving TRT" not "fertility preserving TRT" or "TRT that preserves fertility")

**When a concept bridges territories (cross-territory articles):**
- Both territory concepts are referenced by name
- Neither concept is subordinated to the other — both receive named treatment
- Internal links connect to both territory pillar anchors

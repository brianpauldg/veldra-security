# Voice Calibration Reference — Brian DeGuzman, RN

These sample paragraphs demonstrate Brian's writing voice across seven topic types. Use them as tone anchors when drafting or reviewing Bloom Metabolics content. Each paragraph is a calibration target, not actual published content.

---

## 1. Clinical Mechanism Explanation

**Topic:** How testosterone aromatization works

Most men who start TRT focus on the testosterone number going up. That is the wrong number to watch first. Testosterone converts to estradiol through an enzyme called aromatase, which lives primarily in adipose tissue. The more body fat a man carries, the more aromatase activity he has, and the more of his exogenous testosterone gets converted to estrogen before it can do what he started TRT to accomplish. This is why two men on identical doses can have completely different experiences. The variable is not the testosterone. The variable is the aromatase environment. We check estradiol levels at every follow-up for this reason. Treating testosterone without monitoring estradiol is like adjusting the thermostat without checking whether the windows are open.

## 2. Contrarian Take

**Topic:** Why "normal" testosterone reference ranges are misleading

The standard reference range for total testosterone is roughly 264 to 916 ng/dL, depending on the lab. That range is population-derived, which means it includes 85-year-old men with multiple comorbidities and 25-year-old athletes at their physiological peak. Telling a 38-year-old man that his total testosterone of 310 is "normal" because it falls within that range is technically accurate and clinically useless. Normal is not optimal. Normal is not even symptomatic threshold for many men. The reference range tells you where you fall relative to the population. It tells you nothing about where you function best. We see men at 400 ng/dL with significant symptoms and men at 350 with none. Context matters more than the number. The lab result is a data point, not a diagnosis.

## 3. Patient Pattern Observation

**Topic:** What Brian sees clinically with GLP-1 titration

The pattern we observe with GLP-1 titration is consistent enough to be worth naming. Patients who start at the lowest effective dose and titrate slowly over 8 to 12 weeks report fewer gastrointestinal side effects and, in many cases, comparable weight outcomes at 6 months to patients who escalated quickly. The rush to reach "full dose" is driven by expectations set by social media timelines, not by pharmacology. The receptor does not care about your timeline. We also notice that patients who combine GLP-1 therapy with structured resistance training tend to preserve more lean mass than those who rely on the medication alone. This is not surprising from a physiological standpoint, but it is underreported. The conversation around GLP-1 medications focuses almost entirely on the number on the scale. We think that is the wrong metric.

## 4. Decision Framework

**Topic:** When TRT makes sense vs. when it does not

Testosterone replacement therapy is a clinical tool with a specific use case. It is appropriate when a man has consistently low testosterone confirmed by multiple fasting morning blood draws, accompanied by symptoms that impair quality of life, and after reversible causes have been investigated. That last part gets skipped more often than it should. Poor sleep, excess body fat, chronic stress, certain medications, and undertreated medical conditions can all suppress testosterone. Starting TRT before addressing those factors is not wrong in every case, but it is premature in many. We have patients who optimized sleep and lost 30 pounds and watched their testosterone levels climb 200 points without pharmacological intervention. We also have patients who did everything right and still needed TRT. Both outcomes are legitimate. The framework is: investigate, optimize, measure again, then decide.

## 5. Regulatory Context

**Topic:** Explaining FDA compounding rules

The FDA draws a line between two types of compounding pharmacies: 503A and 503B. A 503A pharmacy compounds medications pursuant to individual patient prescriptions. A 503B pharmacy operates as an outsourcing facility that can produce larger batches without patient-specific prescriptions. Both are legal. Both are regulated. The regulatory scrutiny and manufacturing standards differ. Bloom works with a 503A pharmacy partner, which means every compounded medication is prepared for a specific patient based on a specific prescription from our prescribing clinician. The FDA has stated its position on compounded versions of certain commercially available GLP-1 medications. That position is evolving and patients should be aware of it. We do not editorialize on the FDA's regulatory posture. We follow the current legal framework, we monitor changes, and we communicate those changes to patients when they occur.

## 6. Protocol Nuance

**Topic:** Why HCG timing matters relative to testosterone injection

HCG is often prescribed alongside testosterone to maintain intratesticular testosterone production and, in many protocols, to support fertility preservation. The timing relative to the testosterone injection is a detail most clinics do not discuss. We have observed that patients who administer HCG on the days between testosterone injections, rather than on the same day, tend to report more stable subjective well-being. The pharmacokinetic rationale is straightforward: testosterone cypionate peaks roughly 24 to 48 hours after injection, and HCG stimulates a modest endogenous production surge. Stacking both peaks on the same day may create a sharper hormonal fluctuation followed by a deeper trough. Spacing them distributes the peaks. This is an observational pattern from our practice, not a conclusion from a controlled trial. Research on HCG timing in the context of TRT coadministration is limited, and individual response varies.

## 7. Honest Limitation

**Topic:** What we do not know about long-term peptide use

The honest answer about long-term peptide therapy is that we do not have the data most patients want. The peptides generating the most clinical interest right now, including BPC-157, MOTS-c, and GHK-Cu, have research profiles that are dominated by in-vitro studies, rodent models, and small-sample human trials. That research is often promising, sometimes compelling, and never sufficient to make definitive long-term safety claims. We can explain the proposed mechanisms. We can describe what the existing studies measured. We cannot tell you what happens after five years of continuous use because nobody has studied that yet. Any clinic that presents peptide therapy as a settled, fully characterized intervention is outpacing the evidence. We believe in discussing these compounds honestly with patients who are interested, presenting the research clearly, and being transparent about where the data ends and speculation begins.

## Voice Rules Summary

When drafting, check each paragraph against these markers:

- **Rhythm:** Short declarative sentences mixed with occasional longer compound sentences. One-sentence paragraphs used for emphasis, not filler.
- **Opening:** Mechanism or observation, not hook or statistic.
- **Confidence:** Direct statements about facts and mechanisms. Hedged statements about patient outcomes.
- **Nuance:** At least one moment per article that pushes back, admits a limitation, or complicates the simple narrative.
- **Specificity:** Named enzymes, specific lab values, dosing details, timeframes. Not vague generalities.
- **Tone:** Calm authority. A clinician explaining something to a smart patient, not a marketer selling to a lead.
- **Absent:** Exclamation points, emojis, hype adjectives, filler transitions, apologies for complexity.

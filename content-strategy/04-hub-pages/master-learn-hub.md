## FAQ Section

### Section Header
**Common questions across all pillars**

**Q1: What are normal testosterone levels for men?**

Total testosterone reference ranges typically fall between 264-916 ng/dL for adult men, though optimal ranges and individual symptom thresholds vary. Many clinicians consider levels below 400 ng/dL alongside symptoms as warranting further evaluation. Lab timing, methodology, and individual context all influence interpretation. Read more in our testosterone lab guide.

**Q2: How does GLP-1 medication work for weight loss?**

GLP-1 receptor agonists mimic the incretin hormone GLP-1, slowing gastric emptying, reducing appetite signaling in the brain, and improving insulin sensitivity. The result is typically reduced caloric intake and improved metabolic markers. These medications work best as part of a comprehensive approach that includes nutrition, movement, and behavioral modification.

**Q3: Is testosterone replacement therapy safe?**

TRT has a well-established safety profile when prescribed appropriately and monitored consistently. Standard monitoring includes regular bloodwork for hematocrit, PSA, lipids, and hormone levels. The most common side effects include erythrocytosis, acne, and potential fertility suppression. Individual risk profiles vary and should be assessed with a qualified provider.

**Q4: What is the difference between compounded and brand-name GLP-1 medications?**

Brand-name GLP-1 medications are manufactured by pharmaceutical companies under FDA approval. Compounded versions contain the same active ingredient but are prepared by compounding pharmacies, typically at lower cost. Compounded medications are legal when prepared by licensed pharmacies with valid prescriptions, though they do not undergo the same FDA approval process as brand-name drugs.

**Q5: How much does TRT cost without insurance?**

Cash-pay TRT costs vary significantly by provider and protocol. Testosterone cypionate, the most commonly prescribed form, ranges from $30-80 per month for the medication itself. Total program costs including provider visits, lab work, and ancillary medications typically range from $150-400 per month depending on the practice model and monitoring frequency.

**Q6: Can I take GLP-1 medication if I don't have diabetes?**

Yes. Several GLP-1 receptor agonists are FDA-approved specifically for weight management in non-diabetic adults with a BMI of 30 or above, or 27 or above with at least one weight-related comorbidity. A qualified provider can assess candidacy based on individual health profile.

**Q7: What are peptides and are they legal?**

Peptides are short chains of amino acids that serve as signaling molecules in the body. Their legal status for compounding varies by specific compound and is determined by FDA regulatory frameworks. Some peptides can be compounded through authorized pharmacies with a valid prescription, while others have been restricted. The regulatory landscape is actively evolving.

**Q8: Does TRT cause hair loss?**

TRT can accelerate hair loss in men who are genetically predisposed to androgenetic alopecia. Testosterone itself is not the primary driver — conversion to dihydrotestosterone (DHT) via the 5-alpha reductase enzyme is the mechanism. Men without genetic predisposition typically do not experience TRT-related hair loss.

**Q9: How long does it take for GLP-1 medication to work?**

Most patients notice appetite reduction within the first 1-2 weeks. Measurable weight loss typically begins within 4-8 weeks. Clinical trials show progressive weight loss over 12-16 months, with most patients reaching peak effect between months 12 and 18.

**Q10: What lab work do I need before starting TRT?**

A standard pre-TRT workup includes total and free testosterone (drawn fasting, before 10 AM), LH, FSH, estradiol, CBC with hematocrit, comprehensive metabolic panel, lipid panel, PSA (for men over 40), and thyroid panel. Two morning testosterone draws showing low levels are typically required before initiating therapy.

## Navigation Naming Rationale

The top-level navigation label for this hub is **"The Library"** (not "Resources," "Learn," or "Blog"). This naming was chosen deliberately:

- **OLD label:** "Resources" (or equivalent generic label)
- **NEW label:** "The Library"

**Why "The Library":**
1. Aligns with "The Bloom Peptide Library" (P-TERR-1) — the monograph system uses "Library" as its branded name, and the top-nav label should match
2. Consistent premium clinical reference framing across all content touchpoints
3. Signals depth and permanence — a "library" is a reference destination, not a promotional blog
4. Differentiates from competitor navigation (Hone uses "Resources," Roman uses "Learn," Henry Meds uses "Blog") — Bloom's navigation language itself communicates clinical seriousness

**URL note:** The URL remains **/learn** (not /library) for SEO — "learn" has stronger semantic search value and clearer intent signals. The display name uses "Library" for brand positioning; the URL uses "learn" for search performance. See [01-positioning-and-voice.md](../00-foundation/01-positioning-and-voice.md) §7 for the full naming convention.

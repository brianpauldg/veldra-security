## Page Body Copy

### Introduction

Something changed in metabolic medicine.

For decades, the treatment framework for obesity was simple and largely ineffective: eat less, move more, and if that doesn't work, try harder. That framework failed most patients. Not because they lacked discipline — because the framework didn't account for the neurobiological and hormonal drivers of weight regulation.

GLP-1 receptor agonists represent a genuine paradigm shift. They address the appetite signaling and metabolic regulation pathways that diet and exercise alone cannot override in many patients. The clinical data is strong. The outcomes are measurable. The medications work.

That's the good news.

The rest of the conversation is more complex. These medications have side effects. They require proper titration. They cause muscle loss if not managed. Weight regain after discontinuation is common without a structured exit plan. And the market is flooded with providers who prescribe without monitoring, counsel without expertise, and sell without planning for what happens when the prescription ends.

This guide is organized around three clinical concepts that define how Bloom approaches GLP-1 therapy: lean-mass-preserving GLP-1, the metabolic set-point reset, and 503A compounding standards. Each represents a dimension of clinical care that separates responsible GLP-1 medicine from prescription-mill GLP-1.

**Reviewed by Dr. Michael Napolitano, MD, Medical Director, Bloom Metabolics.**

### Section 1: Lean-Mass-Preserving GLP-1

**Territory: G-TERR-1**

This is the section most GLP-1 providers skip. It might be the most important one.

GLP-1 receptor agonists produce significant weight loss. The STEP trials demonstrated 15-17% average body weight loss with semaglutide 2.4mg over 68 weeks. The SURMOUNT trials showed 20-25% with tirzepatide at maximum doses. These are clinically meaningful outcomes.

But weight is not a single variable. It is composed of fat mass and lean mass. And the data on what kind of weight GLP-1 therapy removes is less comfortable.

Clinical data suggests approximately 25-40% of weight lost on GLP-1 monotherapy is lean mass — muscle, bone mineral density, and other metabolically active tissue. This is not a minor footnote. It has direct implications for metabolic rate, functional capacity, body composition quality, and long-term weight maintenance after discontinuation.

Lean-Mass-Preserving GLP-1 is the protocol philosophy that addresses this head-on. It pairs GLP-1 pharmacotherapy with resistance training, protein optimization, and body composition monitoring (DEXA) to shift the fat-to-lean-mass loss ratio. The goal is not just weight loss — it is fat loss with maximal lean tissue preservation.

**Why lean mass matters:**
- Muscle mass is the primary driver of resting metabolic rate. Losing muscle lowers your caloric needs, making weight maintenance harder after medication stops.
- Sarcopenia in middle-aged and older adults increases fall risk, reduces functional capacity, and worsens metabolic health markers.
- Losing weight but emerging with a higher body fat percentage at a lower weight is not a good outcome.

**The Lean-Mass-Preserving Protocol:**

1. **Resistance Training** — non-negotiable. 3-4 times per week with progressive overload. This provides the mechanical stimulus that signals the body to preserve muscle tissue during caloric deficit.
2. **Protein Intake** — 1.0-1.6 grams per kilogram of body weight daily. On a medication that suppresses appetite, hitting protein targets requires intentional planning.
3. **Adequate Caloric Intake** — moderate deficit (500-750 kcal/day below maintenance), not severe restriction. GLP-1 medications reduce appetite powerfully; some patients undereat to a degree that accelerates muscle loss.
4. **Body Composition Monitoring** — DEXA or bioimpedance at baseline and regular intervals. The scale alone does not tell you what you're losing.

### Section 2: Metabolic Set-Point Reset

**Territory: G-TERR-2**

The STEP 1 extension trial showed that patients who discontinued semaglutide regained approximately two-thirds of their lost weight within one year.

Every GLP-1 provider should be discussing this statistic from day one — not after the patient asks to stop.

**Why weight regain happens:** GLP-1 medications alter the hormonal signaling that drives appetite and metabolic regulation. When the medication is withdrawn, those signals return to their pre-treatment patterns. If no other changes have been made — if the patient hasn't established exercise habits, dietary patterns, and metabolic health foundations — the biological drivers of weight regain are fully intact.

The metabolic set-point reset is the clinical framework for addressing this. It frames GLP-1 therapy not as chronic, indefinite medication but as a time-limited intervention designed to durably shift metabolic set-points — leptin sensitivity, ghrelin signaling, hypothalamic appetite regulation — so that the body defends a lower weight after medication discontinuation.

This is the aspirational clinical goal. The honest caveat: set-point theory is well-established in obesity research, but the degree to which GLP-1 therapy can durably reset set-points varies between patients. Some achieve lasting metabolic adaptation. Others require long-term maintenance dosing. Neither outcome represents failure — they represent the reality that metabolic regulation is biologically mediated and individually variable.

**Bloom's Exit Framework:**

**Phase 1: Active Treatment (Months 1-12)**
GLP-1 medication at therapeutic dose. Concurrent resistance training. Nutritional counseling with protein targets. Metabolic marker monitoring. Body composition tracking. Behavioral habit formation — the habits that need to exist independent of medication.

**Phase 2: Structured Taper (Months 12-18)**
Gradual dose reduction over 2-4 months. Monitoring for appetite return and weight stabilization. Intensified exercise and nutrition support. Hormonal re-evaluation (testosterone, thyroid, metabolic markers). Behavioral assessment: are habits established or medication-dependent?

**Phase 3: Post-Medication Maintenance (Months 18+)**
Ongoing monitoring without medication. Rapid response protocol if weight regain exceeds defined threshold. Option to resume low-dose maintenance if clinically indicated. Continued framework support.

### Section 3: 503A Compounding Standards

**Territory: G-TERR-3**

This is a conversation that requires transparency.

GLP-1 medications have created an access disparity. Brand-name semaglutide (Wegovy) lists at approximately $1,300/month. Brand-name tirzepatide (Zepbound) lists at approximately $1,000/month. Insurance coverage is variable, often requiring prior authorization, step therapy, or documented failure of other interventions. For many patients who would clinically benefit from GLP-1 therapy, the brand-name pathway is financially inaccessible.

Compounded GLP-1 medications — specifically compounded semaglutide prepared by licensed 503A pharmacies — offer a lower-cost pathway to the same active ingredient. This is a legitimate option. It is also one that requires patients to understand the regulatory framework, quality variables, and distinctions from brand-name products.

503A compounding standards refer to the quality, regulatory, and compliance framework that governs compounded medications prepared under Section 503A of the Drug Quality and Security Act. This is not a simple topic, and oversimplifying it does patients a disservice.

**Key distinctions patients must understand:**

- **Compounded is not generic.** Compounded semaglutide is not a generic version of Wegovy. It is a compounded preparation of the same active ingredient, prepared by a pharmacy, pursuant to an individual prescription.
- **Not FDA-approved as a finished product.** Compounded medications do not undergo the same approval process as brand-name drugs. This is a regulatory fact, not a safety judgment — but it is a fact patients deserve to know.
- **Quality varies by pharmacy.** Not all compounding pharmacies are equivalent. Key quality indicators include PCAB accreditation, state board standing, testing protocols for potency and sterility, and history of regulatory actions.
- **503A vs 503B matters.** 503A pharmacies operate under state board oversight with individual prescription requirements. 503B outsourcing facilities operate under FDA oversight with cGMP compliance. The regulatory requirements differ, and so do the quality assurance frameworks.

**Bloom's position:** We offer both brand-name and compounded GLP-1 medications. We are transparent about the distinctions. We work with licensed 503A compounding pharmacies that meet our quality verification standards. We believe the right choice depends on individual clinical circumstances, financial reality, and informed patient preference — not on which option generates more revenue for the provider.

## FAQ Section

**Q1: How much weight can I lose on GLP-1 medication?**

Clinical trials demonstrate average weight loss of 15-22% of body weight over 68-72 weeks with semaglutide 2.4mg, and up to 25% with tirzepatide at maximum doses. Individual results vary significantly based on adherence, lifestyle factors, starting weight, and metabolic profile.

**Q2: What is the difference between semaglutide and tirzepatide?**

Semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist. Tirzepatide is a dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist, targeting two incretin pathways simultaneously. Clinical data suggests tirzepatide may produce greater average weight loss, though individual response varies.

**Q3: What are the side effects of GLP-1 medications?**

The most common side effects are gastrointestinal: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and constipation. These are most pronounced during dose titration and typically diminish over 4-8 weeks. Serious but rare risks include pancreatitis and gallbladder events.

**Q4: Will I regain weight if I stop GLP-1 medication?**

Studies show that most patients regain a significant portion of lost weight within 12 months of discontinuation if no other interventions are in place. This is why Bloom builds the metabolic set-point reset framework into every GLP-1 program — structured exit planning from day one.

**Q5: Does GLP-1 medication cause muscle loss?**

All significant weight loss includes some lean mass loss. Clinical data suggests 25-40% of weight lost on GLP-1 therapy may be lean mass without intervention. Bloom's lean-mass-preserving GLP-1 protocols — resistance training, protein optimization, body composition monitoring — are designed to mitigate this.

**Q6: Is compounded semaglutide safe?**

Compounded semaglutide prepared by licensed 503A pharmacies using validated formulations carries a safety profile consistent with the active ingredient. Quality varies between pharmacies. Bloom works with licensed compounding pharmacies that meet our quality verification standards. Compounded medications are not FDA-approved.

**Q7: How long do I need to take GLP-1 medication?**

Treatment duration is individualized. Most protocols run 12-18 months for initial weight loss, followed by a structured taper (the metabolic set-point reset framework). Some patients may benefit from long-term maintenance dosing.

**Q8: Can I take GLP-1 medication with TRT?**

Yes. The combination may be complementary — obesity suppresses testosterone, and testosterone supports lean mass preservation during weight loss. See the CROSS-001 article on the GLP-1 + Testosterone body composition stack.

**Q9: What happens during dose titration?**

Medication is started at a low dose and increased gradually over 4-8 weeks to reach therapeutic levels. This reduces gastrointestinal side effects by allowing the body to adapt.

**Q10: How much does GLP-1 medication cost without insurance?**

Brand-name GLP-1 medications cost $800-1,300+ per month. Compounded semaglutide through licensed 503A pharmacies is typically $300-500 per month. Bloom program pricing includes medication, provider access, labs, and monitoring.

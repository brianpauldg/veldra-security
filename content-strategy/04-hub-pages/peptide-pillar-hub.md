## Bloom Evidence Tier System — Explainer Block

**[Positioned prominently near top of page, below hero, before monograph index]**

### Section Header
**How we rate the evidence.**

### Explainer Copy

Every monograph in The Bloom Peptide Library includes a Bloom Evidence Tier rating. This is our assessment of the strength and maturity of available evidence for each compound, applied consistently across all monographs.

**Tier 1 — Robust Human Clinical Data**
Multiple completed human clinical trials (Phase II or later) from independent research groups. Published in peer-reviewed journals. Replicated findings. Established safety profile from human use. Example threshold: a compound with 3+ independent human trials and consistent findings.

**Tier 2 — Early or Limited Human Data**
Some human clinical data exists, but it is early-stage (Phase I, pilot studies, case series) or limited in scope. Strong preclinical support exists. The compound has biological plausibility and mechanistic clarity. Safety signals from human use are favorable but the data volume is insufficient for clinical confidence.

**Tier 3 — Preclinical Evidence Only**
Evidence base is primarily in vitro (cell culture) and in vivo (animal models). No completed human clinical trials, or human data is limited to case reports and anecdotal evidence. The compound may have strong preclinical signals, but the translational gap — from animal model to human therapeutic — has not been bridged.

**Tier 4 — Theoretical or Preliminary**
Evidence is sparse, geographically concentrated, or based on theoretical extrapolation from related compounds. Research may come from a single laboratory or a narrow set of investigators. Mechanism may be proposed but not well-characterized. Significant gaps exist in the evidence base.

**Why this matters:** Most peptide content online omits evidence quality entirely, or conflates "biologically active" with "clinically validated." The tier system exists so you can assess claims accurately before they reach you filtered through marketing.

## Page Body Copy

### What Peptides Are

A peptide is a short chain of amino acids — typically between 2 and 50 amino acids in length. Anything longer is generally classified as a protein. The distinction is somewhat arbitrary at the boundary, but functionally, peptides tend to serve as signaling molecules rather than structural ones.

Your body produces thousands of peptides endogenously. Insulin is a peptide. Oxytocin is a peptide. GLP-1 — the hormone that an entire class of weight loss medications is designed to mimic — is a peptide. These are not exotic molecules. They're fundamental components of human physiology.

What distinguishes "peptide therapy" from conventional medicine is the application of exogenous (externally administered) peptides that are not yet FDA-approved as finished pharmaceutical products. These exist in the research literature, often with preclinical data suggesting biological activity, but without the Phase II/III clinical trial data required for FDA drug approval.

**Key distinctions patients should understand:**

- **Endogenous vs. exogenous:** Your body makes many peptides naturally. Whether exogenous administration provides therapeutic benefit beyond what endogenous production achieves is the fundamental research question for most peptides.
- **Preclinical vs. clinical evidence:** Most peptide research is in cell culture or animal models. Human clinical trial data is limited or absent for most compounds. This doesn't invalidate the research — it means the evidence is early-stage.
- **Signaling vs. structural:** Peptides work by triggering cellular responses through receptor binding and signaling cascades. They don't build tissue directly — they tell cells to do things. Understanding this helps calibrate expectations.

---

### The Regulatory Landscape

The peptide compounding landscape changed fundamentally in 2024-2025, and the changes are still unfolding.

The FDA, exercising authority under the Drug Quality and Security Act (DQSA) of 2013, began actively evaluating peptides as bulk drug substances for compounding eligibility. This was not a sudden ban. It was a regulatory process driven by several converging factors: the GLP-1 compounding surge drawing attention to the entire sector, safety concerns from rapid market expansion, and the FDA's evolving enforcement posture on bulk drug substance evaluation.

The FDA established a three-category evaluation framework:
- **Category 1:** Evaluated and permitted for compounding
- **Category 2:** Under active review — status pending
- **Category 3:** Restricted or removed from compounding eligibility

Different peptides landed in different categories, and some have moved between categories as evaluation continues. The 503A and 503B compounding frameworks add another layer — what's restricted through one pathway may remain accessible through the other.

### What to Ask Any Peptide Provider

If you're evaluating peptide therapy from any provider (not Bloom — we don't currently offer peptides), these questions will help you assess quality and credibility:

1. **Which compounding pharmacy do you use, and what is their regulatory standing?** A legitimate provider will name their pharmacy partner.

2. **What evidence supports this peptide for my specific situation?** Ask for citations. Distinguish between "studies show" and "patients report."

3. **What is the evidence level — animal data, human data, or clinical experience?** A provider who doesn't distinguish between these is either uninformed or being intentionally vague.

4. **What monitoring will you do while I'm using this compound?** Peptide therapy without monitoring is not therapy. It's unsupervised self-experimentation with a clinical label.

5. **What are the known risks and what would make you stop treatment?** Any provider who says "there are no risks" is not a provider you should trust.

6. **What is the regulatory status of this compound?** A provider should know whether the peptide they're prescribing is in Category 1, 2, or 3.

7. **What is your exit plan or treatment duration?** Open-ended peptide prescriptions without reassessment points are a flag.

### The Research Outlook

Peptide research is not slowing down. Academic interest is accelerating even as the regulatory framework tightens around clinical access.

**Where the research is heading:**

- **Mitochondrial-derived peptides** (MOTS-c, humanin, SHLP family) continue to attract longevity research interest, with the endogenous origin providing a strong biological rationale.
- **Tissue repair peptides** (BPC-157, TB-500) face regulatory headwinds but retain academic interest in sports medicine and orthopedic research contexts.
- **Growth hormone secretagogues** (CJC-1295, Ipamorelin) remain areas of active investigation for age-related GH decline.
- **Copper peptides** (GHK-Cu) maintain a steady research presence in tissue engineering and regenerative medicine.
- **Anxiolytic peptides** (Selank) represent an area where regional clinical use has outpaced Western clinical trial infrastructure.

**The funding problem:** Many peptides cannot be patented, which means pharmaceutical companies have limited financial incentive to fund the large-scale clinical trials required for FDA drug approval. This creates a structural gap: interesting molecules with biological plausibility but no commercial pathway to the evidence standard patients deserve. This is a legitimate systemic issue, not an excuse for overstating current evidence.

**The realistic timeline:** For most peptides of interest, meaningful human clinical data is 3-5+ years away. Patients who understand this timeline are better equipped to evaluate the claims they encounter today.

## FAQ Section

**Q1: What is The Bloom Peptide Library?**

The Bloom Peptide Library is a monograph system — one comprehensive, honestly rated reference per peptide. Each monograph covers mechanism of action, published research with evidence quality assessment, FDA regulatory status, open research questions, and patient-facing context. All monographs use the Bloom Evidence Tier system and are updated quarterly.

**Q2: What is the Bloom Evidence Tier system?**

A four-tier rating framework that classifies peptide evidence quality. Tier 1 indicates robust human clinical data from multiple independent trials. Tier 2 indicates early or limited human data with strong preclinical support. Tier 3 indicates preclinical evidence only. Tier 4 indicates theoretical or preliminary evidence with significant gaps. Each monograph includes a tier rating with justification.

**Q3: What are peptides?**

Peptides are short chains of amino acids, typically 2-50 amino acids in length. They function as signaling molecules in the body. Many are produced endogenously. Research into exogenous peptide administration explores whether supplementing these molecules can provide therapeutic benefit.

**Q4: Does Bloom Metabolics offer peptide therapy?**

No. Bloom's peptide service line is pending FDA 503A regulatory pathway clarity. We do not currently sell, prescribe, or administer peptides. All peptide content is educational. Patients interested in monograph updates can subscribe to our newsletter.

**Q5: Are peptides legal?**

The term "legal" is imprecise. Peptide availability is determined by FDA compounding regulations. Some can be compounded through authorized pharmacies with valid prescriptions. Others have been restricted. The regulatory landscape is actively evolving. See The 2026 Peptide Regulatory Map for current compound-by-compound status.

**Q6: What is the FDA's position on peptide compounding?**

The FDA has been actively evaluating peptides as bulk drug substances for compounding eligibility since 2023-2024. Substances are placed in three categories: evaluated and permitted, under active review, and restricted or removed. The process is ongoing.

**Q7: What is the difference between 503A and 503B compounding?**

503A pharmacies prepare medications pursuant to individual patient prescriptions under state board oversight. 503B outsourcing facilities can compound without individual prescriptions but must register with the FDA and follow cGMP standards. The two pathways have different rules regarding which substances can be compounded.

**Q8: Are peptides safe?**

Safety profiles vary significantly between different peptides. Most lack long-term human safety data. Additional variables include compound source quality, compounding pharmacy standards, and clinical oversight. "Peptides" is too broad a category for a single safety assessment. Each monograph includes compound-specific safety information.

**Q9: How often is The Bloom Peptide Library updated?**

Quarterly. Each monograph is reviewed and updated to incorporate new published research, regulatory changes, and evidence tier refinements. The 2026 Peptide Regulatory Map follows the same update schedule. All documents display a prominent "Last Updated" date.

**Q10: How can I stay informed about peptide research and regulation?**

Follow FDA updates through the Federal Register and FDA.gov. Access academic research through PubMed. Subscribe to the Bloom Monograph Updates newsletter for periodic research and regulatory updates. We recommend primary sources over social media for peptide information.

## D.2 Evidence Base Acknowledgment

NAD+ and glutathione therapies for longevity and optimization indications have emerging but limited clinical evidence. The Covering Physician must ensure the following are addressed before prescribing:

1. **No FDA-approved indication.** Neither NAD+ nor glutathione has an FDA-approved indication for the specific uses offered through this service line. The patient must be informed of this status.

2. **Compounded product status.** Both products are compounded formulations prepared by a 503A or 503B pharmacy. Compounded products are not FDA-approved and do not undergo the same premarket review as commercially manufactured drugs. The patient must be informed of the distinction.

3. **Evidence limitations counseling.** The physician must discuss the current state of evidence with the patient, including:
   - Preclinical and early-phase human data that supports biological plausibility
   - The absence of large-scale, placebo-controlled clinical trials for most longevity-related claims
   - The distinction between theoretical mechanism and demonstrated clinical outcome

4. **Informed consent.** The informed consent process for Longevity therapies is defined in [SOP-005, Attachment G](../../SOP-005-synchronous-telehealth-visit/attachments/attachment-G-informed-consent.md). The consent form must include explicit language regarding evidence limitations and compounded product status.

## D.3 Treatment Model

`[CLINICAL — PENDING COVERING PHYSICIAN: Define whether the Longevity service line uses the three-phase Initiation/Titration/Maintenance model described in SOP-007 SS5.2, or a simplified loading/maintenance model, or another approach.]`

Industry-typical context for the physician's consideration:

- **Loading/maintenance model:** Some providers use a higher-frequency loading phase (e.g., daily or multiple times per week for 2--4 weeks) followed by a reduced maintenance frequency. This is more common with NAD+ than glutathione.
- **Continuous maintenance model:** Some providers prescribe a consistent frequency from the outset, without a loading phase.
- **Periodic course model:** Some providers prescribe defined treatment courses (e.g., 8--12 weeks) with reassessment between courses rather than indefinite maintenance.

The physician should specify the treatment model and phase definitions for each therapy.

## D.8 Patient Education

The following patient education topics must be addressed and documented before or at the time of first prescription. Education materials are delivered through the patient portal and reinforced during the synchronous visit per [SOP-005](../../SOP-005-synchronous-telehealth-visit/SOP-005-synchronous-telehealth-visit.md).

### Evidence-Base Transparency

- The patient must understand that NAD+ and glutathione therapies have emerging but limited clinical evidence for the indications discussed.
- No FDA-approved indication exists for the specific uses offered.
- Both products are compounded formulations, not commercially manufactured FDA-approved drugs.
- Individual results vary. The therapies may support the discussed outcomes but cannot be guaranteed to produce specific results.

### Injection Technique

- Self-administration of subcutaneous injection for both NAD+ and glutathione. Reference [SOP-004](../../SOP-004-self-injection-training/SOP-004-self-injection-training.md) if applicable for injection training protocol.
- Injection site rotation to minimize local reactions.
- Proper sharps disposal.

### Storage Requirements

- NAD+ and glutathione compounded formulations typically require refrigeration. Specific storage instructions are provided by the compounding pharmacy on the dispensing label.
- Patient should not use product that has changed color, contains particulate matter, or has passed the beyond-use date assigned by the compounding pharmacy.

### Realistic Expectations Setting

- The physician or clinical team should set expectations regarding onset of perceived benefit, the subjective nature of many reported outcomes, and the importance of ongoing communication regarding efficacy and tolerability.
- Patients should understand that discontinuation is appropriate if the therapy is not providing perceived value after a reasonable trial period, as defined by the physician.
